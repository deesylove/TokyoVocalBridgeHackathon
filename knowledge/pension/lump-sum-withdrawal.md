# Pension Lump-Sum Withdrawal (Dattai Ichijikin)

## Overview

When foreign nationals leave Japan permanently, they can apply to receive a lump-sum refund of their pension contributions, called the "dattai ichijikin" (lump-sum withdrawal payment). This allows you to recover a portion of the pension premiums you paid during your time in Japan, rather than leaving them in the system with no future benefit. This is one of the most important financial steps to take when departing Japan, and it must be done after you leave the country.

## Who Needs This

- **Foreign nationals** who have contributed to the Japanese pension system (either National Pension or Employees' Pension) and are leaving Japan permanently.
- You must meet ALL of the following eligibility requirements:
  - You are not a Japanese national.
  - You have contributed to the Japanese pension system for **6 months or more**.
  - You do **not** have an address registered in Japan (meaning you have submitted your move-out notification and left the country).
  - You have **not** qualified for a Japanese pension benefit (i.e., you have not met the 10-year minimum contribution requirement, or you choose to withdraw rather than keep your credits).
- This applies to both National Pension (kokumin nenkin) and Employees' Pension (kosei nenkin) contributors.

## How Much You Get Back

The refund amount depends on how long you contributed and which system you were in.

### National Pension (Kokumin Nenkin)

- The refund is calculated based on a formula using the monthly premium amount and the number of months you contributed.
- As of recent changes, you can receive a refund for **up to 5 years (60 months)** of contributions. Previously the cap was 3 years (36 months), but this was extended.
- Example: If you contributed for 3 years at approximately 16,980 yen per month, your gross refund would be roughly 500,000-600,000 yen (before tax).

### Employees' Pension (Kosei Nenkin)

- The refund is calculated based on your average standard monthly remuneration and the number of months contributed.
- The formula uses the average of your standard monthly salary over your contribution period, multiplied by a payment rate that corresponds to the number of months contributed (up to 60 months / 5 years).
- Since Employees' Pension premiums are higher than National Pension premiums, the refund amount is generally larger.
- Example: If your average monthly salary was 300,000 yen and you contributed for 3 years, the gross refund could be approximately 700,000-900,000 yen (before tax), though the exact amount depends on the formula rates for that fiscal year.

### Important Notes on the Amount

- If you contributed for more than 5 years, you only get a refund based on 5 years of contributions. The excess is forfeited unless you use a totalization agreement.
- The exact calculation rates are published annually by the Japan Pension Service and change each fiscal year.
- Both National Pension and Employees' Pension withdrawals can be claimed simultaneously if you contributed to both systems at different times.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Before Leaving Japan

1. **Confirm your pension records**. Log into Nenkin Net or visit a pension office (nenkin jimusho) to verify your total contribution months and ensure all payments are recorded correctly.
2. **Locate your pension handbook** (nenkin techo). You will need this for the application. If you have lost it, apply for a reissue at the pension office before you leave.
3. **Note your basic pension number** (kiso nenkin bangou): the 10-digit number on your pension handbook.
4. **Consider appointing a tax representative** (nouzei kanrinin) in Japan. This is optional but highly recommended. A tax representative can file a tax refund claim on your behalf after you leave (see Tax Implications below). This person can be a friend, colleague, tax accountant, or professional service.
5. **Submit a "Notification of Tax Representative" form** (nouzei kanrinin no todokede) to your local tax office before departure if you choose to appoint one.
6. **Submit your move-out notification** (tenshutsu todoke) at your city or ward office when you are ready to leave Japan. This deregisters your address and is a prerequisite for the lump-sum withdrawal application.
7. **Keep copies of all documents**: pension handbook, residence card (front and back), passport pages, and your move-out confirmation.

### After Leaving Japan

8. **Obtain the application form**. Download the "Claim Form for Lump-sum Withdrawal Payment" (dattai ichijikin seikyuusho) from the Japan Pension Service website (nenkin.go.jp). The form is available in English and other languages.
9. **Fill out the application form**. Include:
   - Your basic pension number
   - Your name, date of birth, nationality
   - Your address outside Japan
   - Your bank account details for receiving the payment (must be an account outside Japan, or a Japanese bank account in your name if you maintained one)
   - The country and city where you are living
10. **Gather the required documents**:
    - The completed application form
    - Your pension handbook (nenkin techo) - the original
    - A copy of your passport showing your name, date of birth, nationality, signature, and the departure stamp from Japan (the page showing you left Japan)
    - A document verifying your bank account (bank statement or passbook page showing your name, account number, bank name, branch, and SWIFT code)
    - If claiming to a bank account not in your name, a separate authorization is needed
    - A copy of your residence card (if you still have it; it is usually surrendered at the airport, but a photocopy is helpful)
11. **Mail the application** to:
    - Japan Pension Service (Nihon Nenkin Kikou)
    - Address: 3-5-24 Takaido-nishi, Suginami-ku, Tokyo 168-8505, Japan
    - Send by registered mail or a trackable international mail service.
12. **Wait for processing**. Processing typically takes **3 to 6 months** after the Japan Pension Service receives your application. The payment will be transferred to your designated bank account.
13. **Receive a notification of payment** (shiharai tsuchisho). This document is mailed to your overseas address and shows the gross amount, the tax withheld, and the net amount paid.

## Tax Implications

### Withholding Tax

- A **20.42% income tax** is withheld from your lump-sum withdrawal payment before it is sent to you.
- This means you receive only about 79.58% of the calculated refund amount.

### Getting the Tax Back

- You can claim a refund of most or all of the withheld tax by filing an income tax return in Japan.
- **This is why appointing a tax representative is important**: since you will be outside Japan, your tax representative can file the return on your behalf.
- **How it works**:
  1. After you receive the lump-sum withdrawal payment notification (shiharai tsuchisho), send it to your tax representative in Japan.
  2. Your tax representative files a tax return (kakutei shinkoku) at the tax office, claiming a refund of the overpaid tax.
  3. The refund is sent to your tax representative (or directly to your bank account if specified).
- **Without a tax representative**: You can file the return yourself by mail to the Japanese tax office, but this is more complicated from overseas.
- The refundable amount depends on your specific tax situation for the year you departed.

## Totalization Agreements: An Alternative to Withdrawal

Before claiming the lump-sum withdrawal, consider whether a **totalization agreement** (shakai hoshou kyoutei) between Japan and your home country might be more beneficial.

### What Totalization Agreements Do

- They allow your pension contribution periods in Japan to be combined with contribution periods in your home country.
- This helps you meet the minimum qualification period for pension benefits in either country.
- For example, if you contributed to the Japanese pension for 7 years and to the US pension system for 8 years, the totalization agreement lets both countries count the combined 15 years toward their minimum requirements.

### Countries with Totalization Agreements with Japan

As of the most recent updates, Japan has agreements with the following countries:

- **North America**: United States, Canada
- **Europe**: United Kingdom, Germany, France, Belgium, Netherlands, Czech Republic, Spain, Ireland, Hungary, Luxembourg, Switzerland, Slovakia, Finland, Sweden, Italy
- **Asia-Pacific**: Australia, South Korea, India, China (limited to preventing dual coverage)
- **South America**: Brazil

The list is updated periodically. Check the Japan Pension Service website for the most current information.

### Important Consideration

- **Once you claim the lump-sum withdrawal, your Japanese pension contribution record for the refunded period is erased.** You cannot later use those years under a totalization agreement.
- If you contributed for 10 or more years, you may already qualify for a Japanese pension at age 65 and the lump-sum withdrawal would forfeit that right.
- If you contributed for fewer than 10 years but your home country has a totalization agreement, combining your Japanese and home-country periods might be more valuable long-term than the one-time refund.
- **Recommendation**: If you contributed for a long period (especially close to 10 years) or if your home country has a totalization agreement, consult a pension specialist or the Japan Pension Service before deciding.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Applying before leaving Japan**: You cannot apply while you still have a registered address in Japan. You must submit your move-out notification first and apply after departure.
- **Missing the 2-year deadline**: You must file the application within 2 years of leaving Japan. After that, you lose the right to claim.
- **Not appointing a tax representative**: Without one, you effectively lose the 20.42% tax that is withheld, as it is very difficult to file a Japanese tax return from overseas without representation.
- **Losing the pension handbook**: The original pension handbook is a required document. If you lose it after leaving Japan, the process becomes significantly more complicated. Make copies before you leave.
- **Not making copies of your passport departure stamp**: The passport page showing your exit from Japan is required. Photograph or photocopy it immediately after departure.
- **Withdrawing when totalization would be better**: Once you withdraw, those contribution years are permanently erased. If your country has a totalization agreement and you had significant contributions, you might be giving up a larger long-term benefit.
- **Incorrect bank details**: If your bank account information is wrong, the payment will fail and cause significant delays. Double-check your account number, SWIFT/BIC code, bank name, and branch.
- **Not claiming both pension types**: If you contributed to both National Pension and Employees' Pension at different times, you need to claim both separately. Do not forget to include all contribution periods.
- **Surrendering your residence card at the airport without copying it**: At departure, immigration collects your residence card. Make a photocopy (front and back) before going through immigration.

## Key Deadlines

- **Before departure**: Appoint a tax representative and submit the notification to your local tax office. Submit your move-out notification (tenshutsu todoke) at your city or ward office.
- **At departure**: Copy your passport departure stamp and residence card before surrendering the card at immigration.
- **Within 2 years of leaving Japan**: Deadline to submit the lump-sum withdrawal application. This is a strict deadline with no extensions.
- **Processing time**: Expect 3 to 6 months from the date the Japan Pension Service receives your complete application.
- **Tax refund filing**: Your tax representative should file the tax return in the tax year following your departure. The standard tax filing period is February 16 to March 15, but refund claims can be filed for up to 5 years.
