# Japan's Pension System for Foreign Residents

## Overview

Japan has a mandatory public pension system that covers all residents aged 20 to 59, including foreign nationals. If you live and work in Japan, you are legally required to contribute to the pension system. The system provides retirement income, disability benefits, and survivor benefits. For foreign residents who may not stay in Japan long-term, there are options to withdraw your contributions when you leave or to transfer your pension credits to your home country through totalization agreements.

## Who Needs This

- **All foreign residents aged 20-59** living in Japan, regardless of visa type.
- This includes work visa holders, student visa holders, dependent visa holders, spouse visa holders, and permanent residents.
- There are no exemptions based on nationality or expected length of stay (though students with low income may apply for exemptions or deferrals).

## The Two Main Pension Systems

### 1. National Pension (Kokumin Nenkin)

- **Who it covers**: Self-employed individuals, freelancers, unemployed residents, students, and anyone not enrolled in the Employees' Pension.
- **Category**: These enrollees are classified as "Category 1 insured persons" (dai-ichi-gou hihokensha).
- **Monthly premium**: Approximately 16,980 yen per month (the amount is adjusted slightly each fiscal year).
- **Payment**: You receive monthly payment notices by mail. Pay at convenience stores, banks, or set up automatic deduction. Advance payment for 6 months or a full year gives a small discount.
- **Benefits at retirement**: The full basic pension benefit, after 40 years of contributions, is approximately 795,000 yen per year (roughly 66,000 yen per month). The amount is proportional to the number of years contributed.
- **Minimum qualification**: You need at least 10 years (120 months) of contributions to qualify for pension benefits at retirement age (65).

### 2. Employees' Pension (Kosei Nenkin)

- **Who it covers**: Employees of companies that meet enrollment criteria (generally all incorporated businesses and businesses with 5 or more employees in certain industries).
- **Category**: These enrollees are classified as "Category 2 insured persons" (dai-ni-gou hihokensha).
- **Premium rate**: Approximately 18.3% of your standard monthly salary (hyojun hoshu getsu-gaku), split equally between you and your employer. You pay about 9.15% from your salary.
- **Payment**: Automatically deducted from your salary by your employer.
- **What you get**: The Employees' Pension includes the basic National Pension benefit PLUS an additional earnings-related benefit. The total is significantly higher than National Pension alone.
- **Dependents**: Your spouse (if not working or earning below the threshold) is automatically covered as a "Category 3 insured person" for the National Pension at no additional cost.

## Step-by-Step Process: Enrolling in the Pension System

### If You Are Employed (Kosei Nenkin)

1. **Your employer handles enrollment**. When you start working at a company that participates in the Employees' Pension, your employer will register you.
2. **Premiums are deducted automatically** from your monthly salary.
3. **You will receive a pension handbook** (nenkin techo) or a basic pension number (kiso nenkin bangou). If you already have one from a previous enrollment, provide it to your new employer.
4. **Verify your enrollment** by checking your pay slip for pension deductions or by logging into Nenkin Net.

### If You Are Self-Employed, Freelance, or Unemployed (Kokumin Nenkin)

1. **Go to your local city or ward office** (shiyakusho or kuyakusho) within 14 days of establishing residency.
2. **Visit the pension counter** (nenkin madoguchi or kokumin nenkin madoguchi).
3. **Bring the following documents**:
   - Residence card (zairyu kaado)
   - My Number card or notification letter
   - Passport
   - Bank book or cash card (if setting up automatic payment)
4. **Fill out the enrollment form** (kokumin nenkin kanyu todoke). Staff will assist you.
5. **Receive your pension handbook** (nenkin techo) or be assigned a basic pension number. Keep this safe, as you will need it for all future pension-related procedures.
6. **Payment notices** will be mailed to your address. Set up a payment method.

### If You Switch Between Systems

- When you move from employment to self-employment (or unemployment), you must enroll in National Pension within 14 days at your city or ward office.
- When you start a new job with Employees' Pension, your new employer handles the switch. Your National Pension enrollment automatically ends.

## Pension Handbook (Nenkin Techo)

- A small blue booklet that records your pension number and enrollment history.
- **Keep it safe**. You need it when changing jobs, applying for benefits, or claiming a refund when leaving Japan.
- If you lose it, you can apply for a reissue at your local pension office (nenkin jimusho) or city hall.
- Your basic pension number (kiso nenkin bangou) is a 10-digit number in the format XXXX-XXXXXX. This number stays with you throughout your time in Japan.

## Checking Your Records Online: Nenkin Net

- **Website**: nenkin.go.jp (Japan Pension Service)
- **What you can do**: View your contribution history, check estimated future benefits, verify your enrollment status, and download records.
- **How to register**:
  1. Visit the Nenkin Net website.
  2. Click on the registration page (shinki touroku).
  3. Enter your basic pension number, name, date of birth, and other personal information.
  4. A user ID will be mailed to your registered address within about 5 business days.
  5. Log in with your user ID and set a password.
- **Alternative**: You can also check your records in person at any pension office (nenkin jimusho) by bringing your pension handbook and ID.

## Exemptions and Deferrals

If you have difficulty paying premiums, you may be eligible for exemption or deferral:

- **Full exemption, 3/4 exemption, half exemption, 1/4 exemption**: Based on your previous year's income. Apply at your city or ward office.
- **Student exemption (gakusei noufu tokurei)**: Students aged 20 and over with income below approximately 1.28 million yen can defer payments. Apply at your city or ward office with your student ID.
- **Payment deferral (noufu yuuyo seido)**: For those under 50 with low income. Deferred periods count toward the 10-year qualification period but do not add to your benefit amount unless you make retroactive payments later.
- **Important**: Even if you receive an exemption, the period counts toward the 10-year minimum qualification period (at a reduced rate for benefit calculation). This is better than simply not paying and being uninsured.

## What Happens to Your Pension When You Leave Japan

You have three main options:

1. **Lump-Sum Withdrawal (Dattai Ichijikin)**: Claim a refund of your contributions after leaving Japan. See the separate lump-sum withdrawal guide for details.
2. **Totalization Agreement**: If your home country has a pension totalization agreement with Japan, your Japanese contribution periods may count toward your home country's pension qualification. This can be more valuable than the lump-sum withdrawal for long-term stays.
3. **Leave it**: If you contributed for 10 or more years, you can claim a Japanese pension at age 65 even if you no longer live in Japan. You would apply through the Japan Pension Service.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not enrolling at all**: Some foreign residents ignore pension enrollment, thinking it does not apply to them. Non-enrollment can lead to back-payment demands and complications with visa renewal.
- **Not keeping your pension handbook**: You need this booklet for almost every pension-related procedure, including the lump-sum withdrawal.
- **Not switching from company to national pension when leaving a job**: If there is a gap in your coverage, you will owe back premiums.
- **Claiming the lump-sum withdrawal without considering totalization**: If your country has a totalization agreement with Japan, you may get better long-term value by keeping your pension credits rather than withdrawing. Once you withdraw, those years are erased from your record.
- **Not checking Nenkin Net**: Errors in your records can affect your benefits. Verify your contribution history periodically.
- **Paying late**: Late payments may not count toward your contribution record if they exceed the payment deadline by more than 2 years.

## Key Deadlines

- **14 days**: Deadline to enroll in National Pension after establishing residency or after leaving a company that had Employees' Pension.
- **2 years**: You can make retroactive pension payments for up to 2 years for missed months.
- **10 years**: Minimum contribution period to qualify for pension benefits at retirement.
- **2 years after leaving Japan**: Deadline to apply for the lump-sum withdrawal.
- **Every April**: Pension premium amounts and rules are updated for the new fiscal year.
- **Age 60**: Mandatory contribution period ends (though you can voluntarily continue).
- **Age 65**: Standard age to begin receiving pension benefits.
