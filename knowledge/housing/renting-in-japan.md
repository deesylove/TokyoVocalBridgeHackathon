# Renting an Apartment in Japan as a Foreigner

## Overview

Renting in Japan is significantly different from renting in most other countries. The system involves several upfront fees that do not exist elsewhere (key money, guarantor company fees), a formal application process that can feel like applying for a loan, and cultural norms around noise, garbage disposal, and neighbor relations that are strictly observed. Understanding this system before you start looking will save you time, money, and frustration. As a foreigner, you face additional challenges: some landlords refuse foreign tenants, language barriers make the process harder, and you will likely need a guarantor company since you do not have a Japanese guarantor.

## Who Needs This

- Foreign professionals relocating to Japan who need to find their own housing (not provided by employer)
- Anyone whose employer-provided housing is temporary and needs to transition to private rental
- Spouse/dependent visa holders setting up a household
- Students seeking off-campus housing
- Anyone currently in a share house or temporary housing looking to move to a private apartment

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Understand the Cost Structure

Renting in Japan requires a large upfront payment, typically equivalent to 4 to 6 months of rent. Here is what each component means:

- **Shikikin (敷金 / Security Deposit)**: Usually 1-2 months' rent. Refundable at move-out, minus cleaning and repair costs. Some properties have zero shikikin, but this often means higher rent or a non-refundable cleaning fee.
- **Reikin (礼金 / Key Money)**: Usually 0-2 months' rent. This is a non-refundable "gift" to the landlord. It is a traditional custom with no equivalent in most countries. Many newer listings and foreigner-friendly properties offer zero reikin.
- **Chuukai Tesuuryou (仲介手数料 / Agent/Brokerage Fee)**: Usually 1 month's rent plus tax (10% consumption tax). This goes to the real estate agent. Some agents charge half a month or offer discounts.
- **Hoshounin Kaisha (保証人会社 / Guarantor Company Fee)**: Usually 0.5-1 month's rent as an initial fee, with annual renewal fees of 10,000-20,000 yen. Since most foreigners cannot provide a Japanese personal guarantor (rentai hoshounin), you will almost certainly need to use a guarantor company. This company guarantees your rent payment to the landlord.
- **First Month's Rent**: Paid in advance. If you move in mid-month, you pay a prorated amount for the remaining days plus the next full month.
- **Fire Insurance (Kasai Hoken)**: Required. Usually 15,000-20,000 yen for a 2-year policy. Your agent will typically arrange this.
- **Lock Change Fee (Kagi Koukan)**: Usually 15,000-25,000 yen. Changing locks between tenants is standard practice.
- **Cleaning Fee**: Some contracts include a fixed cleaning fee (typically 30,000-50,000 yen for a 1K/1LDK) charged at move-in or deducted from your deposit at move-out.

**Example total for a 1K apartment at 80,000 yen/month:**
| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Shikikin (1 month) | 80,000 yen |
| Reikin (1 month) | 80,000 yen |
| Agent fee (1 month + tax) | 88,000 yen |
| Guarantor company (0.5 month) | 40,000 yen |
| First month's rent | 80,000 yen |
| Fire insurance | 18,000 yen |
| Lock change | 20,000 yen |
| **Total** | **406,000 yen** |

This is approximately 5 months' rent paid upfront before you move in.

### Step 2: Determine Your Budget and Location

- A general rule: your monthly rent should be no more than one-third of your monthly gross income. Landlords and guarantor companies will check this.
- Research neighborhoods based on your commute, lifestyle needs, and budget. Rent varies dramatically by area. In Tokyo, central wards (Shibuya, Minato, Shinjuku) are most expensive. Areas along less popular train lines or slightly outside the city center offer much better value.
- Decide on apartment size. Japanese apartments use a room-number system:
  - **1R (One Room)**: A single room with a small kitchen area. No separate kitchen.
  - **1K (One Kitchen)**: One room plus a separate small kitchen.
  - **1DK (One Dining-Kitchen)**: One room plus a combined dining area and kitchen.
  - **1LDK (One Living-Dining-Kitchen)**: One room plus a living/dining/kitchen area. This is the most comfortable single-person or couple layout.
  - **2LDK, 3LDK, etc.**: Multiple bedrooms plus living/dining/kitchen. For families.

### Step 3: Search for Properties

**Online search portals (mostly in Japanese, but usable with browser translation):**
- **Suumo (suumo.jp)**: Japan's largest rental listing site. Comprehensive but entirely in Japanese. Use Google Translate or a Japanese-speaking friend.
- **Homes.co.jp**: Another major listing site. Similar to Suumo.
- **At Home (athome.co.jp)**: Third major portal.

**Foreigner-friendly search options:**
- **GaijinPot Apartments (apartments.gaijinpot.com)**: English-language listings specifically for foreigners. Smaller selection but all listed properties accept foreign tenants.
- **Real Estate Japan (realestate.co.jp/en)**: English-language listings.
- **Plaza Homes (plaza-homes.co.jp)**: Established agency with English-speaking staff. Focuses on mid-to-high-end properties.
- **Oakhouse / Sakura House**: Specialize in share houses and furnished apartments for foreigners. Good as temporary housing while you search for a permanent apartment.
- **Village House (villagehouse.jp)**: Budget-friendly properties, many accept foreigners. No key money, no deposit, no agent fee at many properties.

**Working with a real estate agent (fudousan-ya):**
- You can visit a local real estate agency in person. Agents have access to listings not always shown online.
- Foreigner-friendly agencies include: GTN (Global Trust Networks), Fontana, Best Estate, PLAZA HOMES, and agencies recommended by your employer's HR.
- Tell the agent your budget, preferred area, desired apartment size, and move-in date. They will search their listings and arrange viewings.

### Step 4: View Properties

- The agent will arrange viewings (naiken). You can typically view multiple properties in one day.
- During viewings, check: water pressure, hot water, condition of walls and floors, sun exposure, noise from neighbors and the street, proximity to the nearest station, nearby convenience stores and supermarkets.
- Take photos and notes. You may be viewing many apartments and they will blur together.
- Ask about garbage disposal rules, building rules, and whether the building has other foreign tenants (a sign that the landlord is foreigner-friendly).
- If you find an apartment you want, tell the agent quickly. Popular properties are taken fast.

### Step 5: Apply for the Apartment

The application (moushikomi) process is thorough:

1. **Fill out the application form**: Your name, nationality, visa type, employer, annual income, length of employment, and emergency contact information.
2. **Provide supporting documents**:
   - Residence card (copy)
   - Passport (copy)
   - Proof of income: Recent pay slips (usually 3 months) or employment certificate (zaishoku shomeisho) from your employer. Some landlords require your annual tax withholding statement (gensen choushu hyou).
   - Employer letter: Confirming your employment, position, and salary. Your HR department can provide this.
   - Emergency contact in Japan: A colleague, friend, or your employer's HR contact.
3. **Guarantor company application**: You will separately apply to a guarantor company (hoshounin kaisha). They run their own screening, checking your income, employment stability, and sometimes credit history. Common guarantor companies include GTN, Casa, JID, and Nihon Safety.
4. **Wait for screening (shinsa)**: The landlord and guarantor company review your application. This takes 3 days to 2 weeks. During this period, the guarantor company may call your employer or emergency contact to verify information. They may also call you directly.
5. **Receive approval or rejection**: If approved, you proceed to contract signing. If rejected, your agent will help you find another property. Rejection reasons are not usually disclosed. Common reasons for foreigners include: landlord preference for Japanese tenants, insufficient income, or short time at current employer.

### Step 6: Sign the Contract

- The contract signing (keiyaku) usually takes place at the real estate agency.
- The agent is legally required to read the "Important Matters Explanation" (juuyou jikkou setsumeisho) to you before you sign. This is a detailed document describing the property, lease terms, and rules. It is read aloud in Japanese. If you do not speak Japanese, bring a translator or ask if the agency can provide an English translation.
- Review key terms: rent amount, payment due date (usually the 27th of the previous month), lease duration (typically 2 years), renewal conditions, penalties for early termination, and prohibited actions.
- Sign or stamp (inkan) the contract and the important matters document.
- Pay all upfront fees. Payment is usually by bank transfer before or on the day of contract signing.
- Receive your keys and move-in documents.

### Step 7: Move In and Set Up Utilities

After signing and receiving your keys:

1. **Electricity**: Usually activates by flipping the breaker. You may need to call the power company (or sign up online) to register the account in your name. Your contract documents will list the power company.
2. **Gas**: Requires an in-person activation visit from the gas company. You must be home during the appointment. Call the gas company listed in your move-in documents to schedule. They will check for leaks and turn on the gas. This is non-negotiable — you cannot use gas until they visit.
3. **Water**: Call the local water utility to start service. Sometimes it is already running and you just need to register.
4. **Internet**: You will need to arrange your own internet. Options include fiber (hikari) through NTT, au Hikari, NURO Hikari, or SoftBank Hikari. Installation takes 2-4 weeks and requires an installation appointment. Pocket WiFi or home routers (from WiMAX or Rakuten) are faster to set up if you need immediate internet.
5. **Ward office address change**: Go to your local ward or city office within 14 days of moving to register your new address. This updates your residence card and juminhyo. Bring your residence card, passport, and your new address.

### Step 8: Understand Lease Terms and Renewal

- Standard leases are 2 years (2-nen keiyaku).
- Most leases auto-renew (jidou koushin). At renewal, you may be charged a renewal fee (koushinryou) of 0.5-1 month's rent. The guarantor company also charges an annual renewal fee.
- Rent increases at renewal are possible but uncommon in Japan unless the property has been significantly improved.
- To end your lease, you must provide written notice (taikyoku todoke) usually 1 to 2 months before your intended move-out date. Check your contract for the exact notice period.
- At move-out, a property inspection will be conducted. Normal wear and tear is the landlord's responsibility, but damage beyond normal use will be deducted from your security deposit. Smoking damage, pet damage, and large stains typically result in deductions.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Underestimating upfront costs**: Budget 4-6 months' rent as an upfront lump sum. Many foreigners are shocked by this amount.
- **Not having income proof ready**: Gather pay slips and an employer letter before you start looking. The application process moves fast once you find a place.
- **Ignoring the guarantor company requirement**: Almost all private rentals require either a Japanese personal guarantor or a guarantor company. Factor the guarantor company fee into your costs.
- **Applying to landlords who do not accept foreigners**: Some listings specify "Japanese only." Your agent should filter these out, but if you are searching on Suumo yourself, look for the phrase "gaikokuseki OK" (外国籍OK) or ask your agent to confirm.
- **Not reading the contract carefully**: The contract is in Japanese and contains important rules about noise, pets, subletting, and modifications. Get a translation of key sections.
- **Missing the rent payment date**: Rent is typically due on the 27th of the month before (e.g., March rent is due February 27th). Late payment can trigger guarantor company involvement and damage your rental history.
- **Not giving proper notice before moving out**: Failing to give the required 1-2 months' notice means you will pay rent for those months even if you have already moved out.
- **Damaging the property without reporting**: Report any damage (wall holes, broken fixtures) promptly. Unreported damage costs more to fix at move-out.
- **Garbage disposal violations**: Each area has strict rules about which garbage goes out on which day, and how it must be sorted and bagged. Violating these rules can lead to complaints from neighbors and warnings from your landlord.

## Key Deadlines

- **14 days after moving**: Register your new address at the ward/city office. This is a legal requirement under the Residential Basic Book Act. Failure to register can result in a fine of up to 50,000 yen.
- **Rent payment**: Usually due on the 25th-27th of the month before the rental month. Set up automatic bank transfer (jidou furikae) to avoid missing payments.
- **Lease renewal**: Your agent or landlord will contact you 1-3 months before the 2-year lease expires about renewal. If you plan to move out at the end of the lease, give notice per your contract terms.
- **Move-out notice**: 1-2 months before your desired move-out date (check your specific contract). Written notice is required.
- **Gas activation appointment**: Schedule this before your move-in date so you have hot water and cooking gas from day one.

## Useful Phrases

- **Chintai (ちんたい / 賃貸)**: Rental (as in rental property). Pronounced "chin-tai."
- **Yachin (やちん / 家賃)**: Rent (monthly payment). Pronounced "yah-chin."
- **Shikikin (しききん / 敷金)**: Security deposit. Pronounced "shee-kee-kin."
- **Reikin (れいきん / 礼金)**: Key money (non-refundable). Pronounced "ray-kin."
- **Chuukai tesuuryou (ちゅうかいてすうりょう / 仲介手数料)**: Brokerage/agent fee. Pronounced "choo-kai teh-soo-ryoh."
- **Hoshounin (ほしょうにん / 保証人)**: Guarantor. Pronounced "hoh-shoh-neen."
- **Hoshounin kaisha (保証人会社)**: Guarantor company. Pronounced "hoh-shoh-neen kai-shah."
- **Fudousan-ya (ふどうさんや / 不動産屋)**: Real estate agency. Pronounced "foo-doh-sahn-yah."
- **Naiken (ないけん / 内見)**: Property viewing. Pronounced "nai-ken."
- **Moushikomi (もうしこみ / 申し込み)**: Application. Pronounced "moh-shee-koh-mee."
- **Keiyaku (けいやく / 契約)**: Contract. Pronounced "kay-yah-koo."
- **Koushin (こうしん / 更新)**: Renewal. Pronounced "koh-shin."
- **Taikyoku (たいきょく / 退去)**: Move-out/vacating. Pronounced "tai-kyo."
- **Madori (まどり / 間取り)**: Floor plan/layout. Pronounced "mah-doh-ree."
- **Eki chika (えきちか / 駅近)**: Close to the train station. Pronounced "eh-kee chee-kah."
- **Juuyou jikkou setsumeisho (重要事項説明書)**: Important matters explanation document. Pronounced "joo-yoh jee-koh seh-tsoo-may-shoh."
