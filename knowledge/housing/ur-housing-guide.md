# UR Housing: A Foreigner-Friendly Alternative for Renting in Japan

## Overview

UR (Urban Renaissance Agency, formally known as Toshi Saisei Kikou / 都市再生機構) is a government-backed housing agency that manages a large inventory of rental apartments across Japan. UR housing is one of the best-kept secrets for foreigners renting in Japan because it eliminates many of the most painful parts of the Japanese rental system: there is no key money (reikin), no guarantor required, no agent fee, and no renewal fee. The application process is based purely on objective income criteria rather than landlord discretion, which means nationality-based rejection does not happen. If you meet the income requirements, you are approved.

## Who Needs This

- Foreign professionals who want to avoid the high upfront costs of private rentals (no key money, no agent fee, no guarantor fee)
- Anyone who has been rejected by private landlords due to nationality
- Families needing larger apartments (UR units tend to be more spacious than comparable private rentals)
- Anyone who wants a straightforward, transparent rental process without hidden fees
- People who do not have a Japanese personal guarantor and want to avoid guarantor company fees
- Long-term residents who want to avoid paying renewal fees every 2 years

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Understand What UR Offers

**What is included:**
- No reikin (key money): You save 1-2 months' rent compared to a typical private rental.
- No guarantor required: Neither a personal guarantor (rentai hoshounin) nor a guarantor company (hoshounin kaisha) is needed. This is a major advantage for foreigners.
- No agent fee: You apply directly through UR. There is no brokerage fee.
- No renewal fee (koushinryou): UR leases are open-ended. There is no 2-year renewal cycle and no renewal fee. Your lease continues indefinitely until you choose to leave.
- Maintenance is handled by UR: Building common areas, exterior maintenance, and structural repairs are UR's responsibility.
- Transparent and standardized contracts: The terms are the same for everyone. No negotiation needed.

**What you do pay:**
- Security deposit (shikikin): Usually 2 months' rent. This is refundable at move-out minus cleaning and repair costs.
- Monthly rent: Comparable to or slightly below market rate for the area, though some older UR properties are below market.
- Community/shared facility fee (kyouekihi): Usually a modest monthly charge for shared spaces, often included in the listed rent.

**Typical upfront cost for a UR apartment at 80,000 yen/month:**
| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Shikikin (2 months) | 160,000 yen |
| First month's rent | 80,000 yen |
| Fire insurance (optional but recommended) | 15,000 yen |
| **Total** | **255,000 yen** |

Compare this to 400,000+ yen for a similar private rental. You save roughly 150,000-200,000 yen upfront.

### Step 2: Check Eligibility Requirements

UR has objective income-based eligibility criteria. You must meet at least one of the following:

**Option A: Monthly income requirement**
- Your average monthly income must be at least 4 times the monthly rent (for rent under 200,000 yen).
- Example: For an apartment at 80,000 yen/month, you need monthly income of at least 320,000 yen (approximately 3.84 million yen annual).
- Income is verified through recent pay slips, tax documents, or an employer certificate.

**Option B: Savings requirement**
- If your income does not meet the threshold (common for new arrivals who do not have Japanese income history yet), you can qualify with savings.
- You need savings equal to approximately 100 times the monthly rent.
- Example: For an apartment at 80,000 yen/month, you need savings of 8,000,000 yen in a bank account.
- This is verified through a bank balance certificate (zandaka shoumeisho) from your bank.

**Option C: Prepaid rent**
- Some UR offices allow you to prepay rent in advance (usually 1 year) to qualify if you do not meet the income or savings requirements.

**Other requirements:**
- You must have a valid residence card with a period of stay long enough to cover the tenancy.
- You need a registered address in Japan (you can use your current address, even if temporary).
- You must be able to physically visit a UR office for the application.

### Step 3: Search for UR Properties

1. **Visit the UR website**: Go to **ur-net.go.jp**. The site is primarily in Japanese, but you can use browser translation. There is some limited English content.
2. **Use the property search function**: Filter by:
   - Area/prefecture (e.g., Tokyo, Kanagawa, Osaka)
   - Ward or city
   - Number of rooms
   - Rent range
   - Move-in availability (some units are available immediately, others have waiting lists)
3. **Check the "Available Now" listings**: UR properties that are currently vacant and ready for immediate move-in are listed under "kuushitsu jouhou" (空室情報). These are the ones you can apply for right away.
4. **Visit a UR office or housing gallery**: UR operates offices and showrooms in major cities where you can browse listings, ask questions, and get help in person. Locations include:
   - UR Shinjuku Office (Tokyo)
   - UR Ikebukuro Office (Tokyo)
   - UR Yokohama Office
   - UR Osaka Office
   - UR Nagoya Office
5. **Note specific property details**: Write down the property name (danchi name), building number, room number, monthly rent, and any special conditions.

### Step 4: Visit and View the Property

- You can request a viewing (naiken) through the UR website or by visiting a UR office.
- Viewings are usually arranged quickly, often within a few days.
- During the viewing, pay attention to:
  - The overall condition of the unit (UR renovates many units before re-listing, but quality varies)
  - Kitchen and bathroom facilities (older units may have smaller kitchens and unit baths)
  - Laundry hookups (most UR units have a balcony area for a washing machine)
  - Storage space (many UR units have built-in closets / oshiire)
  - The surrounding environment: proximity to the nearest train station, supermarkets, parks
  - The building's general condition and community feel

### Step 5: Apply

1. **Go to a UR office** with the following documents:
   - Residence card
   - Passport
   - Proof of income: Pay slips (3 months), tax certificate (kazei shoumeisho), or employer certificate showing salary. For the savings route, bring a bank balance certificate (zandaka shoumeisho) — request this from your bank a few days in advance.
   - MyNumber notification card or MyNumber card
   - Your personal seal (inkan) or be prepared to sign
2. **Fill out the application form** at the UR office. Staff will assist you.
3. **Income/savings verification**: UR staff will review your documents on the spot or within a few days.
4. **Approval**: If you meet the criteria, approval is typically fast — often same-day or within a few days. There is no subjective screening by a landlord. If you meet the numbers, you are in.
5. **Sign the contract**: Once approved, you sign the lease agreement at the UR office. The contract is standardized and straightforward.
6. **Pay the upfront costs**: Security deposit (2 months' rent) and first month's rent (or prorated amount). Payment is by bank transfer.
7. **Receive your keys**: After payment is confirmed, you receive your keys and can move in on the agreed date.

### Step 6: Move In

- Set up utilities (electricity, gas, water) the same way as a private rental. See the renting guide for details.
- Register your new address at the ward/city office within 14 days.
- UR will provide you with building rules, garbage disposal schedules, and contact information for the management office.
- Introduce yourself to the management office and nearby neighbors if possible. UR complexes often have a community feel.

## Advantages Over Private Rentals

- **Massive upfront savings**: No key money, no agent fee, no guarantor fee. You save 2-3 months' rent upfront compared to a typical private rental.
- **No renewal fees**: Over a 4-year stay, you avoid 1-2 months' rent in renewal fees that private rentals would charge.
- **No nationality discrimination**: UR uses objective income criteria. If you qualify, you are accepted. Period.
- **No guarantor hassle**: Eliminates the need to find a Japanese guarantor or pay a guarantor company.
- **Larger units**: UR apartments are often larger than comparable private rentals. A UR 2LDK might be 55-65 square meters versus 40-50 square meters for a similar private unit.
- **Stable rent**: UR rent increases are rare and modest. You will not face sudden rent hikes.
- **Professional management**: UR manages the building, not an individual landlord. Maintenance requests go through a formal system.
- **Pet-friendly options**: Some UR properties allow pets, which is rare in private Japanese rentals.

## Disadvantages

- **Older buildings**: Many UR properties were built in the 1960s-1980s. While many units have been renovated internally, the building exteriors and common areas can feel dated. Some have been rebuilt or extensively renovated, so quality varies widely.
- **Less central locations**: UR complexes (danchi) are often located in suburban areas or on the outskirts of city centers. Expect a longer commute compared to private apartments closer to downtown. However, some UR properties are in surprisingly central locations — it depends on the area.
- **Limited availability**: Popular UR units (recently renovated, good location, reasonable rent) go fast. You may need to check the website frequently and act quickly.
- **Higher security deposit**: UR typically requires 2 months' deposit versus 1 month at many private rentals. However, UR deposits are handled fairly at move-out.
- **Less variety**: UR floor plans tend to follow a few standard patterns. You will not find the architectural variety of private rentals.
- **Application in person**: You generally must visit a UR office in person to apply. The process is not fully online.
- **Japanese-language process**: While UR staff are often helpful, the application process and all documents are in Japanese. Bringing a Japanese-speaking friend is recommended if your Japanese is limited.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not checking the income requirement before visiting**: Calculate whether you meet the 4x monthly income threshold before spending time looking at properties. If your income is borderline, prepare the savings-based alternative.
- **Not getting a bank balance certificate in advance**: If you are using the savings route, you need a bank balance certificate (zandaka shoumeisho). This can take a few days to obtain from your bank. Request it before your UR office visit.
- **Assuming all UR units are the same**: Quality varies enormously. A renovated UR unit can be comparable to a modern private apartment. An unrenovated unit from the 1970s can feel very dated. Always view before applying.
- **Ignoring the location trade-off**: That 60,000 yen 2LDK might look amazing, but if it requires a 60-minute commute, factor in the time and transport cost. A more expensive UR unit closer to your workplace might be better value overall.
- **Not checking availability regularly**: UR units are listed as they become available. If you are flexible on move-in date, check the website weekly. New listings appear frequently.
- **Forgetting about fire insurance**: UR does not require fire insurance, but it is strongly recommended. If you cause a fire that damages the building, you could be liable for enormous costs without insurance. Budget 15,000-20,000 yen for a 2-year policy.
- **Not reading the community rules**: UR complexes have specific rules about noise, garbage, parking, and use of common areas. Violating these rules can lead to complaints and formal warnings.

## Key Deadlines

- **14 days after moving in**: Register your new address at the ward/city office. Legal requirement.
- **Rent payment**: UR rent is due by the end of the month preceding the rental month (e.g., April rent due by March 31). Set up automatic bank transfer to avoid missing payments.
- **Move-out notice**: UR requires 14 days' notice before moving out. This is shorter than the 1-2 months required by most private rentals, which is another advantage. However, check your specific contract.
- **Key return at move-out**: Return all keys on your move-out day. A UR inspection will be conducted, and your deposit refund (minus any deductions) will be processed.
- **Utility cancellation**: Cancel electricity, gas, and water service before or on your move-out day. Schedule gas shutoff in advance, as the gas company may need to visit.

## Useful Phrases

- **UR Toshi Saisei Kikou (UR都市再生機構)**: Urban Renaissance Agency. Pronounced "yoo-ahr toh-shee sai-say kee-koh."
- **Danchi (だんち / 団地)**: Housing complex/estate (the term commonly used for UR-style apartment blocks). Pronounced "dahn-chee."
- **Kuushitsu (くうしつ / 空室)**: Vacant room/available unit. Pronounced "koo-shee-tsoo."
- **Nyuukyo (にゅうきょ / 入居)**: Moving in/occupancy. Pronounced "nyoo-kyo."
- **Shikikin (しききん / 敷金)**: Security deposit. Pronounced "shee-kee-kin."
- **Koushinryou nashi (更新料なし)**: No renewal fee. Pronounced "koh-shin-ryoh nah-shee."
- **Reikin nashi (礼金なし)**: No key money. Pronounced "ray-kin nah-shee."
- **Hoshounin fuyou (保証人不要)**: No guarantor required. Pronounced "hoh-shoh-neen foo-yoh."
- **Zandaka shoumeisho (ざんだかしょうめいしょ / 残高証明書)**: Bank balance certificate. Pronounced "zahn-dah-kah shoh-may-shoh."
- **Kazei shoumeisho (かぜいしょうめいしょ / 課税証明書)**: Tax certificate (proof of income). Pronounced "kah-zay shoh-may-shoh."
- **Naiken (ないけん / 内見)**: Property viewing. Pronounced "nai-ken."
- **Madori (まどり / 間取り)**: Floor plan. Pronounced "mah-doh-ree."
- **Kanri jimusho (かんりじむしょ / 管理事務所)**: Management office. Pronounced "kahn-ree jee-moo-shoh."
