# Health Insurance in Japan

## Overview

Japan has a universal healthcare system. Every resident, including foreign nationals, is legally required to enroll in a health insurance plan. The system covers approximately 70% of medical costs, meaning you pay only 30% out of pocket for most treatments. Without insurance, you pay the full cost, which can be extremely expensive, and you may face penalties for non-enrollment.

## Who Needs This

- **All foreign residents** registered in Japan with a stay of 3 months or longer must enroll in health insurance.
- This applies regardless of visa type: work visa, student visa, dependent visa, spouse visa, or permanent resident status.
- Even if you have private international insurance, enrollment in the Japanese system is mandatory.

## The Two Main Systems

### 1. Company Health Insurance (Shakai Hoken / Kenpo)

- **Who it covers**: Employees of companies with 5 or more workers, or incorporated businesses of any size.
- **How it works**: Your employer enrolls you automatically when you start work. Premiums are deducted from your salary.
- **Premium calculation**: Approximately 10% of your monthly salary, split roughly 50/50 between you and your employer. You pay about 5%.
- **Coverage**: Includes health insurance and pension (kosei nenkin) as a bundled package.
- **Dependents**: Your spouse and children can be covered at no additional premium, as long as their annual income is below approximately 1.3 million yen.
- **Insurance card**: Your employer provides your insurance card (hokensho), usually within 1-2 weeks of enrollment.

### 2. National Health Insurance / NHI (Kokumin Kenko Hoken)

- **Who it covers**: Self-employed individuals, freelancers, unemployed residents, students, and anyone not covered by company insurance.
- **How it works**: You must enroll yourself at your local city or ward office (shiyakusho / kuyakusho).
- **Premium calculation**: Based on your previous year's income, the number of household members enrolled, and your municipality. Rates vary by city.
- **First year note**: If you just arrived in Japan with no previous Japanese income, your first-year premiums may be quite low (sometimes as low as 2,000-3,000 yen per month). They will increase significantly in the second year once your Japanese income is reported.
- **Payment**: Monthly bills are sent to your address, or you can set up automatic bank transfers (kouza furikae). Some municipalities allow credit card or convenience store payment.

## Step-by-Step Process: Enrolling in NHI

1. **Go to your local city or ward office** (shiyakusho or kuyakusho) within 14 days of establishing residency, or within 14 days of losing company insurance.
2. **Go to the National Health Insurance counter** (kokumin kenko hoken madoguchi).
3. **Present the following documents**:
   - Residence card (zairyu kaado)
   - My Number card or My Number notification letter (mainanbaa kaado)
   - Passport
   - A bank book or card if you want to set up automatic payment (kouza furikae)
   - If switching from company insurance: a certificate of loss of insurance (shikaku soshitsu shomeisho) from your previous employer
4. **Fill out the enrollment application form** (kokumin kenko hoken kanyu todoke). Staff can help you fill it out.
5. **Receive your insurance card** (hokensho). Some offices issue it on the spot; others mail it within 1-2 weeks.
6. **Set up a payment method**. You can pay at convenience stores, by bank transfer, or set up automatic deduction.

## Using Your Insurance

### At Hospitals and Clinics

1. Bring your insurance card (hokensho) every time you visit a medical facility.
2. Present it at the reception desk (uketsuke) before seeing the doctor.
3. You pay 30% of the total medical cost at the payment window after your visit.
4. If you forget your card, you may be asked to pay the full amount (100%). You can get reimbursed later by bringing the receipt and your insurance card to the medical facility or your insurance office.

### Prescriptions

- Japan uses a **separated prescription system**. The doctor writes a prescription (shohousen), and you take it to a separate pharmacy (yakkyoku / chouzai yakkyoku) to get your medication.
- Pharmacies are usually located near hospitals and clinics.
- Your 30% co-pay applies to prescription medications as well.
- Prescriptions are typically valid for 4 days, so fill them promptly.

### What Is Covered

- Doctor consultations and examinations
- Surgery and hospital stays
- Prescription medications
- Diagnostic tests (blood work, X-rays, MRI, etc.)
- Mental health treatment
- Maternity care (some portions; normal delivery is not covered but complications are)
- Dental care (basic treatments; cosmetic dentistry is not covered)

### What Is NOT Covered

- Cosmetic procedures
- Normal childbirth delivery (a separate lump-sum birth allowance of 500,000 yen is available)
- Certain advanced treatments not approved under the insurance system
- Preventive care and annual checkups (though employers must provide these)
- Vaccinations (some are subsidized for certain groups)

## High-Cost Medical Care Benefit (Kogaku Ryoyo Seido)

If your medical expenses in a single month exceed a certain threshold, you can apply for the **High-Cost Medical Care Benefit** to get the excess amount reimbursed.

- **Income-based thresholds**: The cap depends on your income level. For a standard income earner, the monthly cap is approximately 80,000-90,000 yen.
- **How to apply**: After paying your medical bills, submit a claim to your insurance provider (your employer's health insurance association or your municipal NHI office).
- **Advance limit certificate (gengaku ninteisho)**: If you know in advance you will have high costs (such as a planned surgery), apply for this certificate beforehand. Present it at the hospital, and they will only charge you up to the cap amount, so you do not need to pay the full amount and wait for reimbursement.
- **Where to get the certificate**: For NHI, apply at your city or ward office. For company insurance, apply through your employer's insurance association.

## Emergencies

- **Emergency number**: Call **119** for an ambulance (kyuukyuusha). Ambulance service is free in Japan.
- **What to say**: "Kyuukyuu desu" (This is an emergency). Give your address and describe the situation.
- **At the emergency room**: Bring your insurance card if possible. If not, treatment will still be provided. You can sort out insurance and payment later.
- **After-hours clinics**: Many areas have holiday and night clinics (kyuujitsu/yakan shinryoujo) for non-life-threatening issues outside regular hours. Check your municipality's website for locations.
- **Tokyo Emergency Interpretation Service (HIMAWARI)**: Call 03-5285-8181 for medical interpretation assistance. Available in multiple languages.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not enrolling within 14 days**: If you delay, you will owe back premiums from the date you became eligible, and you will not have coverage during the gap.
- **Letting premiums lapse**: If you stop paying NHI premiums, your insurance card may be replaced with a short-term card or a certificate of qualification, which makes you pay the full cost upfront and seek reimbursement later.
- **Not switching insurance when changing jobs**: If you leave a company, your company insurance ends. You must enroll in NHI within 14 days or enroll in your new employer's plan.
- **Assuming international insurance replaces NHI**: Japan requires enrollment in the national system regardless of private coverage.
- **Not carrying your insurance card**: Always have your hokensho with you. Without it, you may need to pay the full 100% at the time of visit.
- **Ignoring premium bills**: Unpaid premiums accumulate and can lead to asset seizure in extreme cases.

## Key Deadlines

- **14 days** after moving to Japan or after losing company insurance: deadline to enroll in NHI.
- **14 days** to report changes in household members that affect NHI.
- **Monthly premium due dates**: Vary by municipality, typically at the end of each month. Check your payment notice (nofusho).
- **2 years**: You can retroactively claim medical reimbursements for up to 2 years after the treatment date.
- **High-cost medical care benefit claims**: Must be filed within 2 years of the month in which the medical expenses were incurred.
