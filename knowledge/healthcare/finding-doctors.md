# Finding Doctors and Medical Care in Japan

## Overview

Navigating the Japanese medical system as a foreign resident can be challenging due to language barriers and differences in how healthcare is structured compared to other countries. However, there are many resources available to help you find English-speaking doctors, understand the clinic and hospital system, and get the care you need. Japan has a high standard of medical care, and once you understand the system, it is straightforward to use.

## Who Needs This

- All foreign residents in Japan, regardless of visa type.
- Particularly relevant for those who are not fluent in Japanese and need English-speaking medical providers.
- Applies to anyone enrolled in National Health Insurance (NHI) or company health insurance (shakai hoken).

## Resources for Finding English-Speaking Medical Care

### AMDA International Medical Information Center

- **Phone**: 03-6233-9266
- **What they do**: Provide medical information in multiple languages, help you find hospitals and clinics with multilingual staff, and offer telephone interpretation for medical visits.
- **Languages**: English, Chinese, Korean, Spanish, Portuguese, and others depending on day/time.
- **Hours**: Monday to Friday, 10:00-16:00.
- **How to use**: Call and explain what kind of doctor you need and your location. They will suggest nearby facilities with English support.

### JNTO (Japan National Tourism Organization) Medical Institution Search

- **Website**: Available at the JNTO website under "Safe Travel" resources.
- **What it provides**: A searchable database of medical facilities across Japan that accept foreign patients and offer services in English and other languages.
- **Filter options**: Search by area, specialty, and language.

### Other Resources

- **Your city or ward office**: Many municipalities maintain lists of multilingual medical facilities. Ask at the information counter.
- **Embassy or consulate**: Most embassies maintain lists of recommended English-speaking doctors in their jurisdiction.
- **Tokyo Metropolitan Health and Medical Information Center (HIMAWARI)**: 03-5285-8181. Provides health consultation and helps find appropriate medical facilities in Tokyo. Available in English, Chinese, Korean, Spanish, and Thai.
- **NTT Medical Interpretation Service**: Some hospitals participate in telephone interpretation services during consultations.
- **Online expat communities**: Forums and social media groups for foreign residents in your area often have doctor recommendations.

## Hospital vs. Clinic: Understanding the Difference

### Clinic (Shinryojo / Kurinikku)

- **Size**: Small facilities with fewer than 20 beds (many have no beds at all).
- **Role**: Your first point of contact for most medical issues. Equivalent to a general practitioner or family doctor.
- **Specialties**: Many clinics specialize in one area (internal medicine, dermatology, ENT, orthopedics, etc.).
- **Wait times**: Walk-in is common. Wait times can be 30 minutes to 2 hours depending on the clinic and time of day.
- **Cost**: Generally less expensive than hospitals.

### Hospital (Byoin)

- **Size**: Larger facilities with 20 or more beds, multiple departments, and advanced equipment.
- **Role**: Handles more serious conditions, surgeries, and hospitalizations.
- **Referral system**: Many large hospitals require or strongly prefer a referral letter (shoukaijou) from a clinic. If you visit a large hospital without a referral, you may be charged an additional fee of 5,000-7,000 yen or more.
- **Specialties**: Full range of departments including emergency medicine, surgery, and specialized care.

### Which to Visit

- For general illness, cold, flu, minor injury, or a new health concern: visit a **clinic** first.
- For emergencies, serious conditions, or if a clinic doctor refers you: go to a **hospital**.
- For ongoing specialist care: your clinic doctor will write a referral to the appropriate hospital department.

## Step-by-Step Process: Visiting a Doctor

1. **Find a suitable clinic or hospital**. Use the resources listed above. Check if they accept your insurance type and if they have English-speaking staff.
2. **Check the schedule**. Most clinics are open Monday to Saturday mornings and select afternoons. Many are closed on Sundays, national holidays, and Wednesday or Thursday afternoons. Hospital outpatient departments typically accept patients on weekday mornings only for first visits.
3. **Go to the clinic**. Many clinics accept walk-ins without appointments. Some busier or specialized clinics use reservation systems (online or by phone). Arrive early to reduce wait time, especially at popular clinics.
4. **At the reception desk (uketsuke)**:
   - Present your health insurance card (hokensho).
   - If it is your first visit (shoshin), fill out a medical questionnaire (monshin-hyo). This asks about symptoms, medical history, allergies, and current medications. Some clinics have English-language versions; many do not. Consider bringing a friend who speaks Japanese if you are not confident, or prepare notes about your symptoms in Japanese.
5. **Wait to be called**. Your name will be called (usually in the waiting room). Listen for your name.
6. **See the doctor**. Consultations in Japan tend to be brief, often 5-10 minutes. Be prepared and concise about your symptoms. Write down any questions in advance.
7. **Receive your prescription or treatment**. If medication is needed, the doctor will write a prescription (shohousen).
8. **Pay at the payment window (kaikei)**. You pay your 30% co-pay. A typical clinic visit costs 1,000-3,000 yen out of pocket for a basic consultation.
9. **Go to a pharmacy (yakkyoku)**. Take your prescription to a nearby pharmacy. Present your insurance card and the prescription. The pharmacist will fill it and explain how to take the medication. Pay your 30% co-pay for the medication.

## Mental Health Resources for Expats

Mental health care is available under the Japanese insurance system, though finding English-speaking providers can be more challenging.

- **Psychiatry (seishinkai)**: For conditions requiring medication such as depression, anxiety disorders, ADHD, and others. Covered by insurance.
- **Psychosomatic medicine (shinryou naika)**: Treats conditions where physical symptoms are linked to psychological factors. Covered by insurance.
- **Counseling (kaunseringu)**: Talk therapy and counseling. Some counseling is covered by insurance when provided at a psychiatric clinic, but private counseling is often not covered and can cost 8,000-15,000 yen per session.
- **TELL Lifeline**: 03-5774-0992. Free, anonymous English-language counseling and crisis support. Available daily.
- **TELL Counseling**: Professional face-to-face and online counseling in English. Fees apply but sliding scale may be available.
- **Tokyo English Life Line (TELL)**: Offers both crisis support and ongoing professional counseling services in English.
- **Note on medication**: Some psychiatric medications commonly prescribed in other countries (such as certain stimulants for ADHD) are restricted or banned in Japan. Check with your doctor about Japanese regulations before attempting to bring medication into the country or expecting a specific prescription.

## Dental Care

- Dental care is partially covered by Japanese health insurance.
- **Covered**: Basic examinations, cleanings, fillings (metal amalgam), extractions, root canals, basic dentures.
- **Not covered or partially covered**: Cosmetic treatments, ceramic or white fillings for back teeth, orthodontics, dental implants. These are paid out of pocket (jiyuu shinryou) and can be expensive.
- **Finding a dentist**: Search for "shika" (dentist) in your area. English-speaking dental clinics exist in major cities. The same resources listed above (AMDA, embassy lists) can help.
- **Typical cost with insurance**: A basic checkup and cleaning may cost 2,000-4,000 yen out of pocket.

## Annual Health Checkups (Kenko Shindan)

- **Employer obligation**: All employers in Japan are legally required to provide annual health checkups to their employees at no cost to the employee.
- **What it includes**: Height, weight, blood pressure, vision, hearing, blood tests (cholesterol, blood sugar, liver function), urine test, chest X-ray, and ECG (for those over 35 or with risk factors).
- **How it works**: Your employer will arrange a date at a designated clinic or hospital, or organize an on-site checkup at the office.
- **Self-employed / NHI members**: Your municipality offers basic health checkups (tokutei kenshin) for NHI members, usually at reduced cost or free. Check with your city or ward office for schedules and eligible facilities.
- **Results**: You receive a detailed results sheet, typically within 2-4 weeks. Results flagged as abnormal will include a recommendation to see a specialist.
- **Comprehensive checkups (ningen dock)**: A more thorough examination available for an additional fee (typically 30,000-100,000 yen). Covers cancer screenings, abdominal ultrasound, and more detailed tests. Some company insurance plans subsidize part of the cost.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Going to a large hospital without a referral**: You will be charged an extra fee (tokubetsu ryoukin) of 5,000 yen or more. Visit a clinic first unless it is an emergency.
- **Expecting long consultations**: Japanese doctor visits are typically brief. Prepare your questions and symptoms in advance to make the most of the time.
- **Not bringing your insurance card**: You may need to pay 100% of the cost upfront without it.
- **Assuming prescriptions work the same way**: You cannot get refills by calling the pharmacy. You must see the doctor again for a new prescription each time.
- **Expecting specific medications**: Doctors in Japan may prescribe different medications than what you are used to. Some drugs available over the counter in other countries require prescriptions in Japan, and vice versa.
- **Skipping the pharmacy**: Doctors do not typically dispense medications directly. You must go to a separate pharmacy.
- **Not using the medical questionnaire**: If you cannot read the Japanese questionnaire, ask staff for an English version, or bring a pre-written description of your symptoms in Japanese.

## Key Deadlines

- **Annual health checkup**: Employers must provide this once per year. NHI municipal checkups are often available during specific months (check your local schedule; many run from June to October).
- **Prescription validity**: Prescriptions expire 4 days after the date of issue. Fill them promptly.
- **Insurance enrollment**: Must be completed within 14 days of becoming eligible.
- **Medical expense deduction (kakutei shinkoku)**: If your annual out-of-pocket medical expenses exceed 100,000 yen, you can claim a tax deduction when filing your tax return by March 15 of the following year.
