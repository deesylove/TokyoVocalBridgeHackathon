# Income Tax (Shotokuzei) Guide for Foreign Professionals in Japan

## Overview

Income tax in Japan, called **shotokuzei** (所得税 / しょとくぜい), is a national tax levied on individual income. For foreign professionals, understanding your tax residency status is critical because it determines what income Japan can tax and at what rates. Japan uses a self-assessment system, but most salaried employees have taxes withheld at source by their employer, and many never need to file a return thanks to the year-end adjustment system. Misunderstanding your obligations can lead to penalties, double taxation, or missed refund opportunities.

## Who Needs This

- All foreign nationals earning income in Japan, regardless of visa type.
- Holders of work visas (Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services, Highly Skilled Professional, Intra-company Transferee, Business Manager, Instructor, etc.).
- Spouse visa holders who are employed or self-employed.
- Permanent residents earning income in or outside Japan.
- Students or trainees with part-time income (within visa restrictions).

## Tax Residency Status

Your tax obligations depend on your residency classification, not your visa type:

### 1. Non-Resident (hikyojusha / 非居住者)

- **Definition**: You have lived in Japan for less than 1 year and do not have your primary base of living (jusho) in Japan.
- **What is taxed**: Only Japan-sourced income.
- **Tax rate**: Flat 20.42% withholding on most income (no progressive rates, no deductions).

### 2. Non-Permanent Resident (hijunagaku kyojusha / 非永住者)

- **Definition**: You have lived in Japan for some portion of the last 10 years totaling 5 years or less, AND you do not have permanent residence status for immigration purposes.
- **What is taxed**: Japan-sourced income plus any foreign-sourced income that is paid in Japan or remitted to Japan.
- **Tax rate**: Same progressive rates as permanent residents (see below).

### 3. Permanent Resident for Tax Purposes (junagaku kyojusha / 永住者)

- **Definition**: You have lived in Japan for more than 5 of the last 10 years, OR you hold permanent resident immigration status.
- **What is taxed**: Worldwide income, regardless of where it is earned or paid.
- **Tax rate**: Progressive rates (see below).

**Important**: Tax residency is separate from immigration status. You can become a permanent resident for tax purposes without having a permanent resident visa.

## Tax Rates (2024/2025 Tax Year)

Japan uses a progressive tax rate system for national income tax:

| Taxable Income (Annual) | Tax Rate | Deduction Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Up to 1,950,000 yen | 5% | 0 yen |
| 1,950,001 - 3,300,000 yen | 10% | 97,500 yen |
| 3,300,001 - 6,950,000 yen | 20% | 427,500 yen |
| 6,950,001 - 9,000,000 yen | 23% | 636,000 yen |
| 9,000,001 - 18,000,000 yen | 33% | 1,536,000 yen |
| 18,000,001 - 40,000,000 yen | 40% | 2,796,000 yen |
| Over 40,000,000 yen | 45% | 4,796,000 yen |

**Reconstruction Special Income Tax (fukko tokubetsu shotokuzei)**: An additional surtax of **2.1%** is applied on top of your calculated income tax. This funds recovery from the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake and is in effect through 2037.

**Example**: If your income tax is 500,000 yen, you pay an additional 10,500 yen (500,000 x 2.1%) in reconstruction tax, for a total of 510,500 yen.

## How Employment Income Is Taxed

### Withholding at Source (Gensen Choshu / 源泉徴収)

1. Your employer withholds estimated income tax from each monthly paycheck.
2. The withheld amount is based on tax tables that consider your salary level and number of dependents.
3. Bonuses are also subject to withholding at a separate rate.

### Year-End Adjustment (Nenmatsu Chosei / 年末調整)

1. In November or December, your employer asks you to submit forms declaring your deductions (dependents, insurance premiums, etc.).
2. The employer recalculates your actual tax for the year based on your total annual salary and claimed deductions.
3. If too much was withheld during the year, you receive a refund in your December (or January) paycheck. If too little was withheld, the difference is deducted.

**When you do NOT need to file a tax return**: If you have a single employer who performs the year-end adjustment and your only income is employment income from that employer, you generally do not need to file a tax return (kakutei shinkoku). The year-end adjustment completes your tax obligations.

## Step-by-Step Process

### For Salaried Employees (Standard Case)

1. **Obtain your MyNumber**: When you register as a resident in Japan, you are assigned a 12-digit MyNumber (individual number). You will receive a MyNumber notification card by mail. Apply for a MyNumber Card (plastic IC card) at your municipal office if desired.
2. **Submit your MyNumber to your employer**: Required for tax withholding and reporting. Provide a copy of your MyNumber card or notification card plus photo ID.
3. **Submit withholding declaration forms to your employer**: At the start of employment and each year around November, submit:
   - **Kyuyo shotoku-sha no fuyou kojo-tou (shinko) shinkokusho** (扶養控除等(異動)申告書) - Declaration of dependents.
   - **Hoken-ryo kojo shinkokusho** (保険料控除申告書) - Declaration for insurance premium deductions.
   - **Haigusha tokubetsu kojo shinkokusho** (配偶者特別控除申告書) - Declaration for special spouse deduction (if applicable).
4. **Attach proof documents**: Certificates from insurance companies, proof of overseas dependents' relationship and financial support (remittance records, family register translations).
5. **Receive your year-end adjustment result**: Reflected in your December or January paycheck.
6. **Receive your withholding tax slip (Gensen Choshuhyo / 源泉徴収票)**: Your employer issues this by the end of January for the previous year. Keep this document -- you need it if you later file a tax return.

### When You Must File a Tax Return Instead

See the separate **tax-return-filing.md** guide for details. Common triggers:
- You have income from multiple employers.
- Your annual salary exceeds 20 million yen.
- You have side income exceeding 200,000 yen.
- You are self-employed or a freelancer.
- You are leaving Japan permanently mid-year.
- You want to claim deductions not handled by year-end adjustment (medical expenses, foreign tax credit, first-year mortgage deduction).

## Common Deductions

### 1. Social Insurance Premium Deduction (Shakai Hoken-ryo Kojo / 社会保険料控除)

- All premiums you pay for health insurance, pension (kosei nenkin or kokumin nenkin), employment insurance, and nursing care insurance are fully deductible.
- Automatically handled via year-end adjustment for salaried employees.

### 2. Dependent Deduction (Fuyou Kojo / 扶養控除)

- Claim for qualifying dependents (family members with annual income under 480,000 yen).
- Standard deduction: 380,000 yen per general dependent; 630,000 yen for dependents aged 19-22; 480,000 yen for dependents aged 70+.
- **Overseas dependents**: You CAN claim dependents living abroad (e.g., parents or siblings in your home country). Since 2023, stricter rules apply:
  - Dependents aged 16-29 or 70+ must have proof of remittance of at least 380,000 yen per year per dependent.
  - Dependents aged 30-69 are generally NOT eligible unless they have a disability, are students, or receive remittances of 380,000 yen or more.
  - Required documents: proof of family relationship (birth certificate, family register, etc. with Japanese translation), remittance records (bank transfer receipts).

### 3. Medical Expense Deduction (Iryo-hi Kojo / 医療費控除)

- If total medical expenses for you and your household exceed 100,000 yen (or 5% of income if income is under 2 million yen) in a year, the excess is deductible up to 2 million yen.
- Keep all receipts. Can include some overseas medical expenses.
- Must be claimed by filing a tax return (not handled by year-end adjustment).
- Alternative: **Self-medication tax system (serufu medikeshon zeisei)** -- deduct up to 88,000 yen on qualifying OTC drugs if you had a health checkup during the year.

### 4. Hometown Tax / Furusato Nozei (ふるさと納税)

- A system where you make donations to local governments outside your municipality and receive a tax credit (effectively redirecting your residence tax).
- You receive regional specialty gifts in return (food, crafts, etc.).
- **One-stop exception (wanstoppu tokurei)**: If you donate to 5 or fewer municipalities and are a salaried employee not filing a return, you can apply the deduction without filing a tax return by submitting a one-stop form to each municipality.
- If you donate to more than 5 municipalities or file a tax return for other reasons, you must claim it on your return.

### 5. Life Insurance Premium Deduction (Seimei Hoken-ryo Kojo / 生命保険料控除)

- Deduct premiums for life insurance, medical insurance, and individual pension insurance up to specified limits (max approximately 120,000 yen combined).
- Submit certificates from insurance companies during year-end adjustment.

### 6. Spouse Deduction (Haigusha Kojo / 配偶者控除)

- If your spouse's annual income is 480,000 yen or less (roughly 1.03 million yen in gross salary), you can claim up to 380,000 yen deduction.
- Phase-out applies if your own income exceeds 9 million yen.

## National vs. Local Income Tax

Japan has two separate income taxes:

| | National Income Tax (Shotokuzei) | Local Income Tax (Juminzei) |
|---|---|---|
| **Rate** | Progressive 5%-45% + 2.1% surtax | Approximately 10% (prefectural ~4% + municipal ~6%) |
| **Collected by** | National Tax Agency (NTA) | Your city/ward/town office |
| **Timing** | Withheld monthly, settled via year-end adjustment or tax return | Billed the following year (June - May) based on previous year's income |
| **Filing** | Tax return filed Feb 16 - Mar 15 | Usually no separate filing needed; your income tax return or employer's report is used |

See **residence-tax-guide.md** for full details on local income tax.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not understanding tax residency changes**: Your status shifts automatically based on time spent in Japan. If you cross the 5-year threshold, your worldwide income becomes taxable. Plan ahead.
- **Failing to claim overseas dependents**: Many foreign professionals support family abroad but do not claim the deduction because they are unaware or lack documentation. Gather remittance records and translated family relationship documents proactively.
- **Assuming furusato nozei is free money**: The gifts are effectively free, but you must properly file (or use the one-stop system). If you forget, you pay both the donation and your full tax.
- **Ignoring medical expense deductions**: If your household's medical costs (including dental, vision, prescription, and transportation to hospitals) exceed 100,000 yen, file a return to claim it.
- **Not keeping your gensen choshuhyo**: This withholding slip is essential for filing returns, applying for loans, visa renewals, and more. If you lose it, you must ask your employer for a reissue.
- **Overlooking the reconstruction tax**: When calculating expected taxes, remember to add the 2.1% surtax on your income tax amount.
- **Double taxation**: If you are taxed on the same income in your home country and Japan, you may be eligible for a foreign tax credit. Check if a tax treaty exists between Japan and your country.

## Key Deadlines

| Deadline | Event | Consequence of Missing |
|---|---|---|
| **Within 2 weeks of arrival** | Register residence and receive MyNumber notification | Delays in employment setup and tax processing |
| **November - early December** | Submit year-end adjustment forms to employer | You lose deductions for that year; must file a tax return instead |
| **January 31** | Employer issues gensen choshuhyo for previous year | Contact HR if not received |
| **February 16 - March 15** | Tax return filing period (if required) | Late filing penalty (5-15% of unpaid tax) plus delinquency tax (interest) |
| **March 15** | Payment deadline for income tax due | Delinquency tax of approximately 2.4%-8.7% per year on unpaid amount |
| **Before departure date** | File tax return if leaving Japan permanently | Must appoint a tax representative or file before leaving; otherwise penalties apply |
