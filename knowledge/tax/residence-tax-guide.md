# Residence Tax (Juminzei / Shiminzei) Guide for Foreigners in Japan

## Overview

Residence tax, called **juminzei** (住民税 / じゅうみんぜい), is a local tax paid to the municipality (city, ward, town, or village) and prefecture where you are registered as a resident on January 1 of each year. It is sometimes called **shiminzei** (市民税 / しみんぜい, "city tax") in cities or **kumin-zei** (区民税) in Tokyo's special wards. Unlike national income tax which is withheld in real-time, residence tax is calculated on the **previous year's income** and billed the following year. This one-year lag catches many foreigners off guard, especially when they first arrive or when they leave Japan.

## Who Needs This

- All foreign nationals registered as a resident in Japan on January 1 who had income in the previous year.
- Salaried employees on any work visa.
- Self-employed individuals and freelancers.
- Anyone leaving Japan mid-year who had taxable income the previous year.
- Spouse visa holders with income.

**Does NOT apply to**:
- Persons classified as non-residents for tax purposes (not registered in Japan on January 1).
- Persons with income below the tax-free threshold (generally around 1 million yen in gross salary, varying slightly by municipality).

## How Residence Tax Is Calculated

### Rate Structure

Residence tax has two components:

| Component | Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Income-based portion (shotoku-wari)** | ~10% total | Prefectural tax (~4%) + Municipal tax (~6%) |
| **Per-capita portion (kinto-wari)** | ~5,000 yen/year | Fixed amount regardless of income; varies slightly by municipality |

### Calculation Method

1. **Start with previous year's total income** (January 1 - December 31).
2. **Subtract deductions** (similar to national income tax: basic deduction, dependent deduction, social insurance, etc.). Note: deduction amounts for residence tax are slightly lower than for national income tax.
3. **Multiply taxable income by ~10%** for the income-based portion.
4. **Add the per-capita amount** (~5,000 yen).
5. **Subtract any tax credits** (e.g., furusato nozei donations, dividend tax credits, foreign tax credit).

### Rough Estimate

As a rule of thumb, your annual residence tax will be approximately **10% of your previous year's taxable income**. For example, if your taxable income was 4,000,000 yen, expect roughly 400,000 yen in residence tax the following year.

## Why Your First Year May Be Tax-Free

Residence tax is assessed based on your **registered address on January 1** and your **income from the prior calendar year**.

- **Scenario**: You arrive in Japan in April 2025.
  - On January 1, 2025, you were NOT a resident of Japan.
  - Therefore, you owe **no residence tax** for the June 2025 - May 2026 billing period.
  - You earn income from April - December 2025.
  - On January 1, 2026, you ARE a resident of Japan with 2025 income.
  - You will receive your first residence tax bill starting June 2026, based on your 2025 income.

- **First-year surprise**: Your take-home pay may feel higher in the first year because no residence tax is deducted. Budget for it -- the bill will arrive the following year.

## Payment Methods

### Special Collection (Tokubetsu Choshu / 特別徴収)

- **How it works**: Your employer deducts residence tax from your salary in 12 equal monthly installments (June through May of the following year).
- **Who uses this**: Most salaried employees. Employers with employees are generally required to use this method.
- **Advantage**: Automatic, no need to make payments yourself.
- **Your employer receives**: A tax notification from your municipality around May, listing the monthly deduction amounts.

### Ordinary Collection (Futsu Choshu / 普通徴収)

- **How it works**: You receive a tax bill directly from your municipality and pay it yourself.
- **Who uses this**: Self-employed individuals, freelancers, retirees, or employees whose employer does not handle special collection.
- **Payment schedule**: 4 installments per year.

## The 4-Installment Payment Schedule (Ordinary Collection)

| Installment | Due Date | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **1st installment** | End of June | Largest period; includes notification of annual amount |
| **2nd installment** | End of August | |
| **3rd installment** | End of October | |
| **4th installment** | End of January (following year) | |

**Exact due dates** vary by municipality (usually the last business day of the month). Check your tax notice for the specific dates.

**Payment methods** (vary by municipality, but commonly include):
- Bank transfer (auto-debit from your Japanese bank account -- recommended)
- Convenience store payment (using the barcode on your bill, up to 300,000 yen per slip)
- Credit card payment (via municipal website; processing fees may apply)
- Direct payment at municipal office or designated bank
- Smartphone payment apps (PayPay, LINE Pay, etc. -- increasingly available)

## Step-by-Step Process

### For Salaried Employees (Special Collection)

1. **No action required for payment**: Your employer handles everything. They receive your tax notification in May/June.
2. **Review your tax notification**: Your employer will give you a copy of the **juminzei tokubetsu choshu zeigaku kettei tsuchisho** (住民税特別徴収税額決定通知書). Review it for accuracy.
3. **Verify deduction amounts**: Check your June payslip to confirm the new residence tax amount is being deducted (it will replace the previous year's amount).
4. **Keep your notification**: This document is sometimes requested for visa renewals or loan applications as proof of tax payment.

### For Self-Employed / Ordinary Collection

1. **File your national income tax return** by March 15. Your municipality automatically receives the income data.
2. **Receive your tax bill**: Arrives by mail around June. It contains 4 payment slips for each installment.
3. **Set up payment**: Use bank auto-debit for convenience, or mark your calendar for each due date.
4. **Pay each installment on time**: Late payments incur delinquency charges.
5. **Keep payment receipts**: Useful for visa renewals and as proof of tax compliance.

### Changing Payment Method

- If you leave a company mid-year, your employer may switch your remaining balance to ordinary collection. You will then receive a bill from your municipality for the outstanding amount.
- Alternatively, you can request your employer deduct the remaining annual balance from your final paycheck(s) in a lump sum.

## What Happens When You Leave Japan Mid-Year

This is one of the most common issues for foreign professionals:

### The Problem

- Residence tax for year X is based on income earned in year X-1.
- If you were a resident on January 1 of year X, you owe the FULL year's residence tax, even if you leave Japan in February.
- When you leave Japan, you can no longer pay through special collection from your employer.

### Your Options

1. **Pay in full before leaving**: Ask your employer to deduct the remaining annual balance from your final paycheck. This is the simplest option.
2. **Designate a tax representative (nozei kanrinin)**: Appoint someone in Japan to handle your tax payments after you leave (see below).
3. **Pay all installments before departure**: If on ordinary collection, pay all remaining installments at once at the municipal office.

### What Happens If You Do Nothing

- Unpaid residence tax accumulates delinquency charges.
- It can affect your ability to re-enter Japan or renew a visa in the future.
- The municipality may attempt to contact you through available channels.

## How to Designate a Tax Representative (Nozei Kanrinin / 納税管理人)

If you are leaving Japan and still have tax obligations, you should appoint a tax representative:

1. **Choose your representative**: A trusted person residing in Japan -- a friend, colleague, former employer's HR department, or a tax professional (zeirishi).
2. **Obtain the form**: Get the **nozei kanrinin shinkoku-sho** (納税管理人申告書) from your municipal office or download it from the municipality's website.
3. **Fill out the form**: Include your name, current address in Japan, MyNumber, the representative's name and address, and your expected departure date and overseas address.
4. **Submit the form**: File it at your **municipal office** (city/ward/town hall) for residence tax purposes. You may also need to file a separate form at the **tax office (zeimusho)** for national tax purposes.
5. **Timing**: Submit this BEFORE you leave Japan. Ideally 2-4 weeks before departure.
6. **Representative's role**: They will receive tax bills on your behalf, make payments from funds you provide (or from your Japanese bank account if you keep it open), and handle any correspondence with the tax office.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Budget shock in year two**: Many foreigners enjoy a higher take-home pay in their first year, not realizing that residence tax kicks in the following year. Budget an extra ~10% of income for year-two expenses.
- **Leaving Japan without settling residence tax**: If you owe residence tax and leave without paying or appointing a representative, it creates unresolved tax debt. This can complicate future visa applications.
- **Assuming residence tax is included in year-end adjustment**: Year-end adjustment handles only national income tax. Residence tax is a separate obligation billed the following year.
- **Not updating your address**: If you move to a different municipality, make sure you file a move-out notice (tenshutsu todoke) and move-in notice (tennyu todoke). Your new municipality will bill you going forward, but any remaining payments for the current year still go to the old municipality.
- **Confusing billing periods**: The residence tax year runs June to May, not January to December. Your June 2026 bill is for income earned January-December 2025.
- **Ignoring the tax notice**: Some foreigners discard the tax notice thinking it is junk mail. It is a legal tax bill with payment deadlines. If you cannot read it, ask someone to help translate it.
- **Not checking furusato nozei application**: If you did furusato nozei and used the one-stop system, verify that the deductions appear on your residence tax notification in June. If they do not, you may have missed a deadline or your application was not processed.

## Key Deadlines

| Deadline | Event | Consequence of Missing |
|---|---|---|
| **January 1** | Residency determination date | If registered in Japan on this date, you owe residence tax for that fiscal year |
| **March 15** | Tax return filing deadline (for self-employed) | Delays in residence tax calculation; possible penalties |
| **May - early June** | Municipality issues tax notifications | Review promptly for errors |
| **End of June** | 1st installment due (ordinary collection) / Special collection begins | Late payment triggers delinquency surcharge (~2.4%-8.7% annualized) |
| **End of August** | 2nd installment due | Same penalty |
| **End of October** | 3rd installment due | Same penalty |
| **End of January** | 4th installment due | Same penalty |
| **Before departure from Japan** | Settle remaining tax or appoint nozei kanrinin | Unresolved tax debt; complications with future visa applications |
