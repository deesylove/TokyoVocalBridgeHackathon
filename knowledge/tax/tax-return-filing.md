# Tax Return Filing (Kakutei Shinkoku) Guide for Foreigners in Japan

## Overview

A tax return in Japan is called **kakutei shinkoku** (確定申告 / かくていしんこく), meaning "final declaration." While most salaried employees have their taxes handled by their employer through year-end adjustment, certain situations require you to file a return yourself. Filing can also be beneficial even when not required -- for example, to claim a refund for medical expenses or foreign tax credits. For foreign professionals, knowing when and how to file is essential to staying compliant and avoiding double taxation with your home country.

## Who Needs This

### You MUST file a tax return if:

1. **You are self-employed or a freelancer** (sole proprietor / kojin jigyonushi): All business income must be reported.
2. **You have income from two or more employers**: Year-end adjustment only covers one employer. Income from additional jobs must be reported separately.
3. **Your annual employment income exceeds 20 million yen**: Year-end adjustment cannot be performed for high earners; you must file directly.
4. **You have side income exceeding 200,000 yen**: This includes freelance work, rental income, capital gains, cryptocurrency gains, or other miscellaneous income beyond your main salary.
5. **You are leaving Japan permanently**: You must file a return for the current year's income before your departure date (or appoint a tax representative).
6. **You received income without proper withholding**: Some income sources, especially from overseas, may not have Japanese tax withheld.
7. **You are a non-resident with Japan-sourced income not fully covered by withholding**: Certain types of income require a return.

### You SHOULD file a tax return (even if not required) if:

1. **You want to claim a medical expense deduction**: Year-end adjustment does not handle this.
2. **You want to claim a foreign tax credit**: If you paid tax to another country on income also taxed in Japan.
3. **You want to claim overseas dependent deductions you missed**: If you did not submit documentation during year-end adjustment.
4. **You did furusato nozei to more than 5 municipalities** (or did not submit one-stop forms).
5. **You left a job mid-year and did not have year-end adjustment**: You likely overpaid tax and are due a refund.
6. **You had significant unreimbursed medical expenses, disaster losses, or charitable donations**.
7. **You want to claim first-year housing loan (mortgage) deduction**: The first year requires a tax return; subsequent years can be handled by year-end adjustment.

### You do NOT need to file if:

- You have a single employer who performed year-end adjustment, your only income is from that employer, and you have no additional deductions to claim.

## The Filing Period

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Standard filing period** | **February 16 through March 15** of the year following the income year |
| **Income year** | January 1 - December 31 |
| **Example** | For income earned in 2025, file between February 16, 2026 and March 15, 2026 |
| **Refund-only returns** | Can be filed from **January 1** (no need to wait until February 16) |
| **If March 15 falls on a weekend** | Deadline extends to the next business day |

**Exception -- Leaving Japan permanently**: You must file BEFORE your departure date. There is no option to file from abroad unless you appoint a tax representative (nozei kanrinin) in Japan.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Option A: Filing via e-Tax (Electronic Filing)

e-Tax is the National Tax Agency's online filing system. It is the fastest method and allows you to file from home.

1. **Prepare your MyNumber Card**: You need a MyNumber Card (the plastic IC card, not just the paper notification) with registered electronic certificates. If you do not have one, apply at your municipal office (takes 1-2 months).
2. **Get a card reader or compatible smartphone**: You need an IC card reader connected to your computer, OR a smartphone with NFC capability that supports the MyNumber reading function (most recent iPhones and Android phones work).
3. **Access the filing website**: Go to the NTA's **Kakutei Shinkoku Shosho Sakusei Corner** (確定申告書等作成コーナー) at https://www.keisan.nta.go.jp. Parts of the site are available in English, but forms are in Japanese.
4. **Log in with MyNumber Card**: Use your card reader or smartphone to authenticate.
5. **Select your return type**: Choose the appropriate form:
   - **Form A**: Simpler form for employment income, pension, and miscellaneous income only (discontinued from 2023 -- now unified into Form B).
   - **Form B**: The standard form for all income types.
6. **Enter your income**: Input data from your gensen choshuhyo and any other income documents.
7. **Enter your deductions**: Input social insurance, dependents, medical expenses, foreign tax credits, etc.
8. **Review and submit**: The system calculates your tax. Review, then submit electronically.
9. **Pay any tax due**: Via bank transfer, credit card, convenience store, or direct debit.
10. **Save your receipt**: Download and save the electronic receipt confirmation.

### Option B: Filing on Paper at the Tax Office

1. **Locate your jurisdictional tax office (zeimusho / 税務署)**: Based on your registered address. Find it at the NTA website or by calling the NTA hotline (0570-064-000).
2. **Gather all required documents** (see list below).
3. **Visit the tax office**: During the filing period, tax offices set up special filing assistance areas with staff who can help you complete the forms. Arrive early -- lines can be very long, especially in the first and last weeks.
4. **Fill out the return form**: Staff may assist, but assistance is typically in Japanese only. Some tax offices in areas with large foreign populations may have multilingual support or interpreter services.
5. **Submit the form and attachments**: Hand it to the clerk at the submission counter.
6. **Receive a stamped copy**: Ask for a copy stamped with the receipt date for your records (important for visa applications and proof of filing).
7. **Pay any tax due**: At the tax office, bank, or convenience store using the payment slip provided.

### Option C: Filing by Mail

1. Complete the tax return form (download from the NTA website or use the online tool to generate a PDF).
2. Attach all required documents.
3. Mail to your jurisdictional tax office by March 15 (postmark date counts).
4. Include a stamped return envelope if you want a receipt copy mailed back.

## Required Documents

### For All Filers

| Document | Japanese Name | Where to Get It |
|---|---|---|
| **Withholding tax slip** | 源泉徴収票 (gensen choshuhyo) | From your employer(s), issued by end of January |
| **MyNumber Card or notification** | マイナンバーカード | Municipal office; or MyNumber notification + photo ID |
| **Bank account information** | 口座情報 | For refund deposit; bring your bankbook or cash card |
| **Personal seal (inkan)** | 印鑑 | If filing on paper (not always required but recommended) |

### For Specific Deductions

| Situation | Required Documents |
|---|---|
| **Medical expense deduction** | Medical expense itemized statement (iryo-hi no meisaisho); keep receipts for 5 years |
| **Overseas dependent deduction** | Proof of family relationship (translated to Japanese), remittance records showing at least 380,000 yen/year per dependent |
| **Foreign tax credit** | Foreign tax payment certificate or tax return from the other country, proof of income earned abroad |
| **Furusato nozei (if not using one-stop)** | Donation receipts (kifukin uketsuke shomeisho) from each municipality |
| **Life/earthquake insurance deduction** | Premium payment certificates from insurance companies |
| **Housing loan deduction (first year)** | Loan balance certificate, property registration certificate, sale contract, residence certificate |
| **Business income (self-employed)** | Income and expense ledgers, receipts, invoices |

## Common Situations for Foreigners

### 1. Claiming the Foreign Tax Credit (Gaikoku Zei-gaku Kojo / 外国税額控除)

If you paid tax to another country on income that is also taxed in Japan, you can claim a credit to avoid double taxation.

- **Who qualifies**: Permanent residents for tax purposes (worldwide income is taxable) and non-permanent residents on income remitted to Japan that was taxed abroad.
- **How to claim**: Complete the **Foreign Tax Credit Calculation Form** (gaikoku zei-gaku kojo ni kansuru meisaisho) and attach it to your return.
- **Required documents**: Tax payment proof from the foreign country, details of the foreign income, exchange rates used.
- **Limitation**: The credit cannot exceed the portion of your Japanese tax attributable to foreign-sourced income (calculated by formula).
- **Tax treaties**: Japan has tax treaties with many countries that may affect which country has primary taxing rights. Check if a treaty exists with your home country.

### 2. Claiming Overseas Dependent Deductions

If you support family members living outside Japan, you may claim dependent deductions even if you did not submit documents during year-end adjustment.

- **Steps**:
  1. Prepare proof of family relationship (birth certificates, marriage certificates, family register equivalents) with Japanese translations.
  2. Prepare remittance records showing financial support of at least 380,000 yen per dependent per year (bank transfer receipts, wire transfer confirmations).
  3. Enter each dependent on the tax return form.
  4. Attach all supporting documents.

### 3. Medical Expense Deduction for Foreigners

- Medical expenses paid in Japan AND abroad may qualify.
- For overseas medical expenses, you need receipts and a Japanese translation or summary.
- Transportation costs to hospitals (train, bus fare) count as deductible. Keep a log.
- Insurance reimbursements must be subtracted from total expenses.
- **Threshold**: Total qualifying expenses minus insurance reimbursements must exceed 100,000 yen (or 5% of total income if under 2 million yen).
- **Online tool**: The NTA's filing website has a medical expense input tool that calculates the deduction automatically.

### 4. Leaving Japan Permanently (Junnen Shinkoku)

This is one of the most important situations for foreign professionals:

1. **File your return BEFORE you leave Japan**, covering income from January 1 through your departure date.
2. **Or appoint a tax representative (nozei kanrinin)** to file and pay on your behalf after you leave.
3. **What to include**: Prorate your income, deductions, and exemptions for the partial year.
4. **Residence tax**: Remember that residence tax for the current billing year must also be settled (see residence-tax-guide.md).
5. **Timing**: File anytime before departure. Do not wait for the February 16 - March 15 window.
6. **Refunds**: If you overpaid via withholding, the refund can be deposited to your Japanese bank account (keep it open) or to your tax representative's account.

## Getting a Tax Refund (Kanpu / 還付)

You are entitled to a refund if you overpaid taxes through withholding. Common refund scenarios:

- You left employment mid-year and had no year-end adjustment (excess tax was withheld assuming higher annual income).
- You have large deductions (medical expenses, dependents) that were not factored into withholding.
- You qualify for the foreign tax credit.
- You made furusato nozei donations and need to claim income tax portion via return.

### Refund Process

1. **File a refund return (kanpu shinkoku)**: Can be filed from January 1 (you do not need to wait until February 16).
2. **Specify your bank account**: On the return form, provide your Japanese bank account details. The account must be in your name.
3. **Processing time**: Typically 1-2 months after filing. e-Tax returns are processed faster (about 3 weeks).
4. **Notification**: You will receive a **kokuzei kanpu-kin furi-komi tsuchi-sho** (国税還付金振込通知書) by mail confirming the refund amount and deposit date.

## What Happens If You Miss the Deadline

### Filing After March 15 (Late Filing)

| Penalty | Amount |
|---|---|
| **Late filing penalty (mushinkoku kasanzei)** | 15% of unpaid tax (5% if filed within 1 month voluntarily, plus certain conditions met) |
| **Increased penalty for large amounts** | 20% on the portion of unpaid tax exceeding 500,000 yen |
| **Delinquency tax (entaizei)** | Approximately 2.4% annually for the first 2 months, then approximately 8.7% annually thereafter (rates adjusted yearly) |

### Additional Consequences

- **Blue return privileges revoked**: If you are a self-employed filer using the blue return (ao-iro shinkoku) system, two consecutive late filings may result in loss of blue return status and its associated benefits (higher deductions, loss carryforward).
- **Criminal penalties**: Intentional tax evasion can result in fines up to 5 million yen and/or imprisonment up to 5 years. This applies to willful fraud, not honest mistakes.
- **Voluntary late filing**: If you realize you missed the deadline and file voluntarily before the tax office contacts you, the penalty is reduced to 5% (under certain conditions).
- **Amended returns**: If you discover an error after filing, you can file an amended return (shusei shinkoku) to pay additional tax, or a claim for correction (kosei no seikyuu) to get a refund, within 5 years.

### Refund Returns Have a 5-Year Window

If you are filing only to claim a refund (not because you owe additional tax), you have **5 years** from the year after the income year to file. For example, for 2025 income, you can file a refund return until December 31, 2030. There is no late penalty for refund-only returns.

## Step-by-Step Filing Checklist

1. [ ] Determine if you are required to file or if filing would be beneficial.
2. [ ] Collect all gensen choshuhyo from every employer for the tax year.
3. [ ] Gather supporting documents for any deductions you plan to claim.
4. [ ] Prepare your MyNumber Card (for e-Tax) or MyNumber notification plus photo ID (for paper filing).
5. [ ] Choose your filing method: e-Tax, in-person at tax office, or by mail.
6. [ ] If using e-Tax: access the NTA website, authenticate, enter all income and deductions, review, and submit.
7. [ ] If filing in person: visit your jurisdictional tax office during the filing period with all documents.
8. [ ] If you owe tax: pay by March 15 via bank transfer, convenience store, credit card, or at the tax office.
9. [ ] If expecting a refund: confirm your bank account details on the return and wait 3-8 weeks.
10. [ ] Keep copies of your filed return and all receipts for at least 5 years (7 years for blue return filers).

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not realizing you need to file**: If you changed jobs, have side income, or earn over 20 million yen, you must file even if your main employer did year-end adjustment.
- **Filing only for one employer when you had two**: All income must be declared. The tax office cross-references employer filings and will catch discrepancies.
- **Forgetting to file before leaving Japan**: If you leave permanently without filing or appointing a tax representative, you may face penalties and complications with future visa applications.
- **Not claiming available deductions**: Many foreigners leave money on the table by not claiming medical expenses, overseas dependents, or foreign tax credits.
- **Submitting documents in foreign languages only**: The tax office requires Japanese or Japanese-translated documents. Prepare translations in advance.
- **Using the wrong tax office**: You must file at the tax office that has jurisdiction over your registered address, not any convenient location.
- **Not keeping copies**: Always get a stamped receipt copy of your paper return, or save the electronic confirmation from e-Tax. You may need it for visa renewals or official procedures.
- **Cryptocurrency and investment income**: Foreign professionals sometimes forget that crypto gains, stock sales, and other investment income earned worldwide (for permanent tax residents) must be reported.
- **Missing the payment deadline**: Filing on time but paying late still incurs delinquency tax on the unpaid amount.

## Key Deadlines

| Deadline | Event | Consequence of Missing |
|---|---|---|
| **January 1** | Earliest date to file a refund-only return | No consequence; this is an opportunity, not a requirement |
| **January 31** | Employers must issue gensen choshuhyo | Contact HR if you have not received yours |
| **February 16** | Tax return filing period opens | Filing before this date is accepted for refund returns |
| **March 15** | Filing AND payment deadline | Late filing penalty (5-20%), delinquency tax (~2.4-8.7%), possible loss of blue return |
| **Before departure date** | File return if leaving Japan permanently | Must be done before leaving unless tax representative appointed |
| **Within 5 years** | File refund-only returns (retroactively) | After 5 years, you lose the right to claim the refund |
| **Within 5 years** | File a correction claim (kosei no seikyuu) for overpayment | After 5 years, you cannot request a correction |
