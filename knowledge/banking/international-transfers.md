# Sending and Receiving Money Internationally from Japan

## Overview

Moving money into or out of Japan is something most foreign professionals need to do regularly, whether sending money home to family, paying off loans or obligations in their home country, or receiving funds from abroad. Japan's traditional banking system makes international transfers expensive and slow, but modern fintech services like Wise have made the process much more affordable. Understanding your options, the costs involved, and the legal reporting requirements is essential to avoid overpaying fees and staying compliant with Japanese regulations.

## Who Needs This

- Foreign workers sending money to family in their home country (remittances)
- Professionals paying mortgages, loans, or other financial obligations abroad
- Freelancers receiving payment from international clients
- Anyone transferring savings when relocating to or from Japan
- Business owners with international payment needs
- Students receiving financial support from family abroad

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Understand Your Options

There are several ways to send money internationally from Japan. Here are the main methods, ranked roughly by cost-effectiveness:

**Option A: Wise (formerly TransferWise) — Recommended for most people**
- The most popular and cost-effective option among foreigners in Japan.
- Uses the real mid-market exchange rate with a small transparent fee (typically 0.4% to 1.5% depending on the currency pair and amount).
- A transfer of 100,000 yen to USD typically costs around 700-1,500 yen in fees, with no exchange rate markup.
- Transfers usually arrive within 1-3 business days, sometimes same day.
- Supports transfers to most countries and currencies.

**Option B: Japanese bank wire transfer (Gaikoku Sokin)**
- Available at all major banks (MUFG, SMBC, Mizuho, Yucho, etc.).
- Expensive. Typical fees: 2,500 to 6,000 yen per transfer as a sending fee, plus the bank applies its own exchange rate which includes a markup of 1-3 yen per dollar (or equivalent margin for other currencies). Intermediary bank fees of 1,000-3,000 yen may also apply, and the receiving bank may charge an additional fee.
- A transfer of 100,000 yen through a bank can easily cost 5,000-10,000 yen or more in total fees and exchange rate losses.
- Transfers take 3-7 business days.
- Must be done in person at a branch during banking hours (9:00 AM - 3:00 PM weekdays) at most banks, though some offer online international transfers.

**Option C: Revolut**
- Available in Japan since recent years. Offers competitive exchange rates and low fees.
- Free-tier accounts have limits on fee-free exchanges. Paid tiers offer higher limits.
- Good alternative to Wise, especially for multi-currency management.

**Option D: PayPal**
- Can be used for international transfers, but has significant limitations in Japan.
- PayPal Japan accounts have restrictions on personal transfers. Primarily designed for commercial transactions.
- Exchange rates include a markup of 3-4% above the mid-market rate.
- Receiving money via PayPal in Japan and withdrawing to a Japanese bank account incurs fees.
- Not recommended as a primary method for regular remittances.

**Option E: Other services**
- **SBI Remit**: Lower fees than banks, available at some convenience stores. Popular for remittances to Southeast Asia.
- **Western Union**: Available through some partners in Japan. Higher fees but wide global reach.
- **Prestia (SMBC Trust Bank)**: Offers online international transfers with somewhat lower fees than regular SMBC. Good if you already have a Prestia account.
- **Sony Bank**: Offers international transfers with competitive rates due to their multi-currency account structure.

### Step 2: Set Up Wise (Recommended Path)

1. **Download the Wise app** or go to wise.com and create an account.
2. **Verify your identity**: You will need to upload:
   - A photo of your residence card (front and back)
   - A photo of your passport
   - A selfie for identity verification
   - Your MyNumber (Individual Number) — this is mandatory for any financial service in Japan that handles international transfers. You can provide your MyNumber card, MyNumber notification card, or a juminhyo (residence certificate) that shows your MyNumber.
3. **Add your Japanese bank account** as a funding source. You will need your bank name, branch name, account type (usually futsu/ordinary), and account number.
4. **Add a recipient**: Enter the details of the person or account you want to send money to. Requirements vary by country but typically include the recipient's name, bank name, account number, and routing/sort code.
5. **Make your first transfer**: Enter the amount, review the fees and exchange rate, and confirm. Fund the transfer from your Japanese bank account via domestic bank transfer (furikomi) to Wise's Japanese bank account.
6. **Wait for the transfer**: Most transfers complete within 1-3 business days. You can track progress in the app.

### Step 3: Making a Bank Wire Transfer (Alternative Path)

If you prefer or need to use your bank:

1. **Visit your bank branch** during business hours with your passbook, cash card, residence card, and MyNumber.
2. **Fill out the Gaikoku Sokin Yoshiki (Foreign Remittance Form)**. You will need:
   - Recipient's full name (exactly as it appears on their bank account)
   - Recipient's bank name, branch, and address
   - Recipient's account number and SWIFT/BIC code
   - Purpose of the transfer (e.g., "living expenses for family," "loan repayment," "personal savings transfer")
   - Your relationship to the recipient
3. **Choose who pays intermediary fees**: You can select OUR (you pay all fees), BEN (recipient pays), or SHA (fees are shared). OUR is most expensive but ensures the recipient gets the full amount.
4. **Confirm the exchange rate and fees** before finalizing. Ask the staff to show you the total cost.
5. **Keep your receipt**. You will need it for your records and potentially for tax purposes.

### Step 4: Receiving Money from Abroad

To receive an international transfer into your Japanese bank account:

1. **Provide the sender with your bank details**:
   - Bank name (in English)
   - Branch name and branch code
   - SWIFT/BIC code (each bank has one; for example, MUFG's is BOTKJPJT, SMBC's is SMBCJPJT)
   - Your account number
   - Your name (as registered on the account, usually in katakana and/or romaji)
2. **Be aware of incoming fees**: Your bank may charge a receiving fee of 1,500-2,500 yen for incoming international transfers. Intermediary banks may also deduct fees.
3. **The bank may contact you**: For transfers above a certain amount, your bank may call or send a letter asking you to verify the purpose of the incoming funds. This is routine anti-money-laundering procedure.
4. **Using Wise to receive**: If you have a Wise multi-currency account, you can receive funds in USD, EUR, GBP, and other currencies using Wise's local bank details in those countries, then convert and transfer to your Japanese account at better rates.

### Step 5: Understand Reporting Requirements

Japan has strict reporting rules for international money transfers:

- **Transfers over 1,000,000 yen (approximately $7,000-$9,000 USD)**: Your bank is required to automatically report these to the Bank of Japan. You do not need to do anything yourself, but be aware that large transfers are monitored.
- **Transfers over 30,000,000 yen (approximately $200,000-$270,000 USD)**: You are personally required to file a report with the Bank of Japan within 20 days of the transfer. This applies to both sending and receiving. The form is called the "Shihon Torihiki Todokede" (Capital Transaction Report). Your bank can provide the form, or you can submit it through the Bank of Japan's website.
- **Multiple smaller transfers**: If you regularly send amounts just under the reporting threshold, this pattern may attract scrutiny. Banks monitor for "structuring" (deliberately splitting transfers to avoid reporting).
- **Tax implications**: Money you send abroad is not taxable (it is your own money being moved). However, if you receive money from abroad that constitutes income (freelance payments, rental income, etc.), it is taxable in Japan and must be declared on your tax return.

### Step 6: Compare Costs Before Each Transfer

Before making any transfer, compare costs across services:

- Check the exchange rate each service offers against the mid-market rate (Google "JPY to [currency]" for the mid-market rate).
- Add up all fees: sending fee, exchange rate markup, intermediary fees, and receiving fees.
- For a 100,000 yen transfer to the US, typical total costs are approximately:
  - Wise: 700-1,500 yen total
  - Revolut: 800-1,500 yen total (depending on plan)
  - Prestia online: 3,000-4,000 yen total
  - Major bank branch: 5,000-10,000 yen total
  - PayPal: 4,000-6,000 yen total (due to exchange rate markup)

## Common Pitfalls

- **Using your bank for regular remittances**: If you send money home monthly, using your bank's wire transfer service could cost you 60,000-120,000 yen per year in fees alone. Switch to Wise or a similar service.
- **Not providing MyNumber to Wise**: Your Wise account will be limited or suspended if you do not complete MyNumber verification. Do this during initial setup.
- **Incorrect recipient details**: If the recipient's name does not exactly match their bank records, the transfer may be rejected or delayed. Double-check every detail. SWIFT codes and account numbers must be exact.
- **Forgetting the purpose of transfer**: Japanese banks require you to state the purpose of every international transfer. "Personal savings" or "family support" are standard answers. Vague or suspicious answers can delay your transfer.
- **Not keeping records**: Keep receipts and confirmation emails for all international transfers. You may need them for tax filing or if questioned by your bank.
- **Assuming PayPal works like it does at home**: PayPal Japan has significant restrictions on personal transfers. It is not a reliable option for regular remittances.
- **Ignoring the 30M yen reporting requirement**: If you are transferring large sums (selling property abroad, inheritance, large bonus), failing to report transfers over 30 million yen is a legal violation.
- **Exchange rate timing**: Rates fluctuate constantly. For large transfers, even a small rate difference matters. Check rates at different times of day, and consider setting up rate alerts on Wise.

## Key Deadlines

- **30,000,000 yen+ transfer reporting**: Must be filed with the Bank of Japan within 20 days of the transfer date. Failure to report can result in fines.
- **Tax filing for overseas income**: If you receive taxable income from abroad, it must be declared during the annual tax filing period (February 16 to March 15 of the following year).
- **Wise MyNumber verification**: Must be completed before your account is fully functional. If you set up Wise before receiving your MyNumber, complete the verification as soon as you have it to avoid account suspension.
- **Bank reporting for 1M yen+ transfers**: Automatic and immediate. No action required from you, but expect your bank to ask questions about the purpose if this is your first large transfer.

## Useful Phrases

- **Gaikoku sokin (がいこくそうきん / 外国送金)**: International remittance/wire transfer. Pronounced "gai-koh-koo soh-kin."
- **Sokin (そうきん / 送金)**: Remittance or money transfer. Pronounced "soh-kin."
- **Kawase reeto (かわせレート / 為替レート)**: Exchange rate. Pronounced "kah-wah-seh reh-toh."
- **Tesuuryou (てすうりょう / 手数料)**: Fee/commission. Pronounced "teh-soo-ryoh."
- **Uketori nin (うけとりにん / 受取人)**: Recipient/beneficiary. Pronounced "oo-keh-toh-ree neen."
- **Sokin mokuteki (そうきんもくてき / 送金目的)**: Purpose of remittance. Pronounced "soh-kin moh-koo-teh-kee."
- **Kazoku e no shienn (かぞくへのしえん / 家族への支援)**: "Support for family" (common purpose of transfer). Pronounced "kah-zoh-koo eh no shee-en."
- **Jiko shikin no idou (じこしきんのいどう / 自己資金の移動)**: "Transfer of personal funds." Pronounced "jee-koh shee-kin no ee-doh."
- **Chuukai ginkou (ちゅうかいぎんこう / 仲介銀行)**: Intermediary/correspondent bank. Pronounced "choo-kai gin-koh."
- **Shihon torihiki todokede (しほんとりひきとどけで / 資本取引届出)**: Capital transaction report (for transfers over 30M yen). Pronounced "shee-hon toh-ree-hee-kee toh-doh-keh-deh."
