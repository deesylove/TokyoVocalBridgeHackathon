# Opening a Bank Account in Japan as a Foreigner

## Overview

A Japanese bank account is essential for daily life in Japan. Your employer will almost certainly require one for salary payments, and most recurring bills (rent, utilities, phone) are paid via automatic bank transfers (furikomi). Without a local account, you will struggle to pay for housing, receive your salary, or set up basic services. Japan remains a cash-heavy society, so reliable ATM access also matters.

## Who Needs This

- All foreign nationals living and working in Japan on any visa type (work visa, spouse visa, student visa, working holiday, etc.)
- New arrivals who need to receive salary payments
- Anyone setting up utilities, rent payments, or other recurring transfers
- Freelancers and business owners who need a Japanese account for invoicing

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Understand the Residency Requirement

Major Japanese banks — MUFG (Mitsubishi UFJ), SMBC (Sumitomo Mitsui), and Mizuho — generally require you to have lived in Japan for at least six months before they will open a full-service account. If you have been in Japan for less than six months, these banks may refuse your application outright or offer a restricted account (no international transfer capabilities, no online banking, limited ATM access).

This policy is based on anti-money-laundering regulations and internal bank risk policies. It is not a legal requirement, so some branches may be flexible, especially if your employer intervenes.

### Step 2: Choose a Bank

**If you have been in Japan less than six months, consider these foreigner-friendly options:**

- **JP Post Bank (Yucho Ginko)**: The most accessible option. Post offices are everywhere, and Yucho is generally willing to open accounts for new arrivals. The account has limited features compared to major banks, but it works for salary deposits and domestic transfers. Many employers accept Yucho for payroll.
- **Shinsei Bank**: Known for being foreigner-friendly. Offers English online banking, no ATM fees at convenience store ATMs during certain hours, and relatively easy account opening. Can sometimes be opened within the first six months.
- **SBI Sumishin Net Bank**: An online-only bank with a straightforward application process. English support is limited, but the app is usable. Good for people comfortable with online banking.
- **Sony Bank**: Offers multi-currency accounts and English support. Popular among foreigners who need to manage multiple currencies.
- **Prestia (SMBC Trust Bank)**: Formerly Citibank Japan. Designed for international customers, with full English support, multi-currency accounts, and international transfer capabilities. Higher minimum balances and fees, but excellent for professionals who need international banking features.

**If you have been in Japan more than six months:**

- **MUFG**: Japan's largest bank. Extensive branch and ATM network. Limited English support. Reliable for salary and bill payments.
- **SMBC**: Second largest. Similar to MUFG in terms of coverage. Some branches in major cities have English-speaking staff.
- **Mizuho**: Third largest. Good branch network. English support varies by branch.

### Step 3: Gather Required Documents

You will need the following when you visit the bank:

1. **Residence Card (Zairyu Card)**: This is your primary ID in Japan. It must be valid and show your current address. If you recently moved and your address is not updated, go to your ward office first.
2. **Passport**: Some banks ask for this as secondary identification.
3. **MyNumber Notification Card or MyNumber Card**: Banks are required to collect your MyNumber (Individual Number) for tax reporting. Bring either your MyNumber notification letter (the paper card you received when you registered at the ward office) or your plastic MyNumber Card.
4. **Inkan (Personal Seal) or Signature**: Most major banks now accept signatures for foreigners, but some branches still prefer an inkan. If you have one registered at your ward office (jitsuin), bring it. If not, a signature will usually suffice. Shinsei, Sony Bank, and Prestia all accept signatures.
5. **Proof of Address**: Your residence card typically serves this purpose. Some banks may also accept a utility bill or ward office residence certificate (juminhyo).
6. **Proof of Employment or Student Status**: Not always required, but having your employee ID, work contract, or student ID can help, especially if you have been in Japan less than six months.
7. **Your Phone Number**: A Japanese phone number is usually required. If you do not have one yet, get a SIM card first.
8. **Initial Deposit**: Usually 1,000 yen to 10,000 yen in cash. Some banks accept zero, but bringing a small amount is recommended.

### Step 4: Visit a Branch

- Go to your chosen bank branch during business hours (typically Monday-Friday, 9:00 AM to 3:00 PM). Some banks have extended hours until 5:00 PM.
- Take a numbered ticket at the entrance. You may need to indicate that you want to open a new account (shinki kouza kaisestu).
- When called to the counter, tell the staff you want to open an account. If your Japanese is limited, say: "Kouza wo hirakitai desu" (I want to open an account).
- Fill out the application form. Staff will usually help you. You will need to write your name (in katakana and romaji), address, phone number, date of birth, and occupation.
- Provide your documents. The staff will photocopy your residence card and record your MyNumber.
- Register your seal (inkan) or signature as your account verification method.
- Make your initial deposit.

### Step 5: Receive Your Cash Card

- In most cases, you will NOT receive your bank card (cash card) on the same day. It will be mailed to your registered address within 1 to 2 weeks.
- You will receive a temporary passbook (tsucho) on the spot at most major banks. This small booklet records your transactions and can be updated at ATMs.
- Your cash card is used for ATM withdrawals and deposits. It is not a debit card by default.

### Step 6: Set Up a Debit Card (Optional)

- A cash card and a debit card are different in Japan. The cash card only works at ATMs. A debit card (often branded as Visa Debit or JCB Debit) can be used for purchases at stores and online.
- Some banks issue a combined cash/debit card. Others require a separate application for a debit card.
- Shinsei Bank and Sony Bank issue combined cards by default. At MUFG, SMBC, and Mizuho, you may need to request a debit card separately.

### Step 7: Set Up Online Banking

- Most banks offer online banking (Internet Banking), but setup can be confusing.
- You will typically receive login credentials by mail after your account is opened, or you can register through the bank's app.
- **English-language online banking** is available at: Shinsei Bank, Prestia, Sony Bank, and (partially) SBI Sumishin.
- MUFG, SMBC, and Mizuho online banking is primarily in Japanese, though some have partial English interfaces or apps.
- Online banking allows you to make domestic transfers (furikomi), check balances, and set up automatic payments.

### Step 8: Set Up ATM Access

- Your bank's cash card works at that bank's ATMs and at many convenience store ATMs.
- **7-Eleven ATMs (Seven Bank)**: Accept most Japanese bank cards and many international cards. Available 24/7 in most locations. This is often the easiest ATM option for foreigners.
- **Lawson ATMs**: Also widely compatible.
- **FamilyMart ATMs (E-net)**: Compatible with most bank cards.
- ATM fees vary by bank and time of day. Withdrawals outside banking hours (evenings, weekends, holidays) typically incur fees of 110 to 220 yen per transaction.
- Shinsei Bank offers free convenience store ATM withdrawals depending on your account tier.

### Step 9: Employer-Required Accounts

- Your employer may require you to open an account at a specific bank for payroll. This is common and sometimes non-negotiable.
- If your employer banks with MUFG, they may insist you open an MUFG account. In this case, the employer can sometimes assist with the six-month residency issue by providing a letter or having HR accompany you to the branch.
- Ask your employer early about their banking requirements so you can plan accordingly.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Going to a major bank too early**: If you have been in Japan less than six months, MUFG/SMBC/Mizuho will likely turn you away. Start with Yucho or Shinsei to avoid wasted trips.
- **Not bringing MyNumber**: Banks are legally required to collect this. If you do not have your MyNumber notification or card, you will be turned away.
- **Address mismatch**: If the address on your residence card does not match where you currently live, the bank will not open your account. Update your address at the ward office first.
- **No Japanese phone number**: Almost all banks require a Japanese phone number. A prepaid SIM or MVNO number works.
- **Expecting same-day card issuance**: Your cash card arrives by mail. Do not expect to walk out of the bank with a working card.
- **Confusing cash card with debit card**: Your cash card only works at ATMs. You need a separate debit card or credit card for purchases.
- **Branch staff refusing foreigners**: Some individual branch staff are uncomfortable serving foreign customers. If refused at one branch, try another branch of the same bank, or bring a Japanese-speaking friend.
- **Not setting up online banking promptly**: You will need online banking for transfers and bill payments. Set it up as soon as you receive your credentials.

## Key Deadlines

- **Before your first payday**: You need an account open and the account number provided to your employer before your first salary payment. Start the process as soon as you arrive, since the card takes 1-2 weeks to arrive.
- **MyNumber submission**: Banks must collect your MyNumber. If you do not provide it when opening the account, you may be asked to submit it later, and failure to do so can result in account restrictions.
- **Address changes**: If you move, you must update your address with your bank. Failing to do so can cause your card reissue or important mail to go to the wrong address. Update within a few weeks of moving.

## Useful Phrases

- **Kouza (こうざ / 口座)**: Bank account. Pronounced "koh-zah."
- **Kouza wo hirakitai desu (口座を開きたいです)**: "I want to open an account." Pronounced "koh-zah oh hee-rah-kee-tai des."
- **Tsuchou (つうちょう / 通帳)**: Bankbook/passbook. Pronounced "tsoo-choh."
- **Kyasshu kaado (キャッシュカード)**: Cash card (ATM card). Pronounced "kyash-shoo kah-doh."
- **Furikomi (ふりこみ / 振込)**: Bank transfer. Pronounced "foo-ree-koh-mee."
- **Inkan (いんかん / 印鑑)**: Personal seal/stamp. Pronounced "in-kahn."
- **Zairyu kaado (ざいりゅうカード / 在留カード)**: Residence card. Pronounced "zai-ryoo kah-doh."
- **Mai nanbaa (マイナンバー)**: MyNumber (Individual Number). Pronounced "mai nahm-bah."
- **Genkin (げんきん / 現金)**: Cash. Pronounced "gen-kin."
- **Hikiotoshi (ひきおとし / 引き落とし)**: Automatic withdrawal/direct debit. Pronounced "hee-kee-oh-toh-shee."
- **Risoku (りそく / 利息)**: Interest. Pronounced "ree-soh-koo."
- **Zanndaka (ざんだか / 残高)**: Account balance. Pronounced "zahn-dah-kah."
