# First Steps After Arriving in Japan on a Work Visa

## Overview

Your first 14 days in Japan are critical. Several registrations are legally required within strict deadlines, and the order you complete them matters because each step depends on the previous one. Missing deadlines can result in fines, complications with your visa, or difficulty accessing essential services like banking and healthcare. This guide walks you through everything in the correct sequence.

## Who Needs This

- Anyone arriving in Japan on a work visa (Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services, Intra-company Transferee, Highly Skilled Professional, Instructor, etc.)
- Dependents arriving on a Dependent visa (haigusha visa)
- This guide assumes you are arriving for the first time or re-entering after a period of residence cancellation

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Airport Immigration (Day 1)

**What happens:** Upon landing at Narita, Haneda, Kansai, or another international airport, you will go through immigration control.

**Required documents:**
- Valid passport with your work visa sticker (or Certificate of Eligibility if applying for landing permission)
- Certificate of Eligibility (COE) -- the original document your employer sponsored
- Disembarkation card (usually distributed on the plane or available at immigration counters)

**Process:**
1. Proceed to the immigration counter for foreign nationals.
2. Present your passport, COE, and disembarkation card.
3. Your fingerprints and photograph will be taken.
4. The immigration officer will issue your **Residence Card (Zairyu Card)** on the spot at major airports (Narita, Haneda, Kansai, Chubu, New Chitose, Hiroshima, Fukuoka). At smaller airports, you will receive a stamp in your passport and must pick up your Residence Card later at your local immigration office.
5. The Residence Card will show your name, nationality, status of residence, period of stay, and a photo.
6. **Important:** Confirm all details on the card are correct before leaving the counter. Errors are much harder to fix later.

**Useful tip:** If you need to activate the re-entry permit function, tell the immigration officer you want "minashi sainyukoku kyoka" (deemed re-entry permission).

### Step 2: Register Your Address at City Hall (Within 14 Days)

**What happens:** You must register your residential address at your local municipal office (shiyakusho for cities, kuyakusho for Tokyo wards, or machiyakuba for towns).

**Deadline:** Within 14 days of establishing your address (moving into your apartment or residence).

**Required documents:**
- Residence Card (Zairyu Card)
- Passport
- Your new address in Japanese (ask your employer or landlord to write it down)

**Process:**
1. Go to the residents' registration counter (jumin-ka or shimin-ka) at your local city/ward office.
2. Fill out the **Moving-In Notification (tennyuu todoke)** form. Staff can help you fill it out.
3. Submit the form along with your Residence Card and passport.
4. The clerk will update your Residence Card with your new address printed on the back.
5. You will be registered in the **Basic Resident Register (juminhyo)**.
6. If you have family members with you, register everyone at the same time.

**Why this matters:** Your juminhyo (resident registration) is the foundation for almost everything else -- bank accounts, phone contracts, health insurance, pension, and MyNumber all depend on it.

### Step 3: Apply for MyNumber (Happens Automatically After Step 2)

**What happens:** When you register at city hall, you are automatically enrolled in the MyNumber system. MyNumber is a 12-digit personal identification number used for tax, social security, and disaster response.

**Process:**
1. After registering your address, the city office will mail your **MyNumber Notification** to your registered address within approximately 2-3 weeks.
2. The notification letter contains your 12-digit MyNumber.
3. You can optionally apply for a **MyNumber Card (kojin bangou card)** -- a plastic IC card with your photo. This is increasingly useful for online government services and can serve as ID.
4. To get the physical MyNumber Card, apply online (using the QR code on the notification), by mail, or at a designated photo booth. Processing takes about 1-2 months.
5. When the card is ready, you will receive a pickup notification. Go to your city hall with your Residence Card and passport to collect it.

**Why this matters:** Your employer needs your MyNumber for tax withholding and social insurance enrollment. You can give them the 12-digit number from the notification letter before the physical card arrives.

### Step 4: Enroll in Health Insurance (National Health Insurance or Employer-Based)

**What happens:** Japan requires all residents to have health insurance. Your enrollment path depends on your employer.

**Option A -- Employer-based health insurance (shakai hoken):**
- If your company enrolls you in their health insurance society (kenpo kumiai) or the Japan Health Insurance Association (kyokai kenpo), your employer handles the paperwork.
- Enrollment typically happens on your start date.
- Your employer will give you a health insurance card (hokensho) within a few weeks of starting.
- Premiums are deducted from your salary (roughly 5% of your gross salary, with employer matching the same amount).

**Option B -- National Health Insurance (kokumin kenko hoken):**
- If your employer does not provide shakai hoken (common for smaller companies, part-time, or freelance), you must enroll at your city/ward office.
- Go to the National Health Insurance counter at city hall.
- Required documents: Residence Card, passport, MyNumber notification (if available).
- Premiums are based on your previous year's income. In your first year, premiums may be low or based on a minimum amount since you had no Japanese income.

**Deadline:** Enroll as soon as possible after address registration. Coverage is retroactive to your registration date, but if you delay enrollment, you may face a lump-sum payment for missed months.

### Step 5: Enroll in the Pension System (National Pension or Employee Pension)

**What happens:** All residents aged 20-59 must participate in Japan's pension system.

**Option A -- Employee Pension (kosei nenkin):**
- If your employer enrolls you in shakai hoken, you are automatically enrolled in kosei nenkin.
- Premiums (about 9.15% of salary) are deducted from your paycheck with employer matching.

**Option B -- National Pension (kokumin nenkin):**
- If not enrolled through an employer, go to the pension counter at city hall or your local pension office (nenkin jimusho).
- Monthly premium is a flat rate (approximately 16,980 yen per month as of 2025).

**Important note for those who leave Japan:** If you leave Japan permanently and were enrolled in the pension system for 6 months or more but fewer than 10 years, you can claim a **Lump-Sum Withdrawal Payment (dattai ichijikin)** after departure. Keep your pension handbook (nenkin techo) and records.

### Step 6: Open a Bank Account (After 2 Weeks to 6 Months)

**What happens:** Most major banks require you to have lived in Japan for at least 6 months before opening an account. However, several options exist for newcomers.

**Immediate options (within the first few weeks):**
- **Japan Post Bank (Yucho Ginko):** Often the most foreigner-friendly. Some branches allow account opening shortly after address registration. Bring your Residence Card, passport, and proof of address.
- **Shinsei Bank / SBI Shinsei Bank:** Offers online banking in English and may accept applications from newer residents.
- **Wise (TransferWise) or Revolut:** Digital accounts that can receive Japanese yen to a Japanese account number. Useful as a bridge until you open a traditional account.

**After 6 months:**
- Major banks like MUFG, SMBC, and Mizuho will generally accept your application.
- Bring: Residence Card, passport, MyNumber notification or card, inkan (personal seal) or signature, and initial deposit.

**Your employer may help:** Many companies help new foreign employees open a bank account or can pay your initial salary by cash or check until your account is ready.

**Tip:** Ask your employer to write a letter confirming your employment -- this can help when applying at banks that are reluctant to open accounts for new residents.

### Step 7: Get a Phone Number (SIM Card or Mobile Contract)

**What happens:** A Japanese phone number is needed for many services including bank verification, apartment applications, and work communication.

**Immediate options:**
- **Prepaid SIM cards:** Available at airport shops and electronics stores (Bic Camera, Yodobashi Camera). Brands include IIJmio, Mobal, and Sakura Mobile. Some offer voice + data, others data only.
- **Mobal:** Specifically targets foreigners and does not require a long residency history. Available online.

**Full contract (after bank account is set up):**
- Major carriers: docomo, au (KDDI), SoftBank.
- Budget carriers (MVNOs): Y!mobile, UQ Mobile, LINE Mobile, IIJmio, Rakuten Mobile.
- Required: Residence Card, bank account or credit card for payment, MyNumber Card (some carriers).
- Rakuten Mobile is popular among foreigners for its English-friendly signup and competitive pricing.

### Step 8: Additional Registrations and Setup (First Month)

**Additional tasks to complete in your first few weeks:**

1. **Register your seal (inkan touroku):** If you plan to sign major contracts (apartment leases, car purchases), register a personal seal at city hall. Many foreigners can use a signature instead, but some processes still require an inkan.

2. **Get a commuter pass (teikiken):** If commuting by train, your employer may provide a commuter allowance. Buy a commuter pass at the train station using your route.

3. **Notify your embassy:** Some countries recommend or require you to register with your embassy or consulate in Japan.

4. **Set up utilities:** If not already done by your employer or landlord, set up electricity, gas, water, and internet at your residence.

5. **Obtain a Japanese driver's license (if needed):** If you plan to drive, you must convert your foreign license at the driver's license center (unten menkyo senta). Some countries have reciprocal agreements; others require a written and/or practical test.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Missing the 14-day address registration deadline.** This is a legal requirement. Failure to register can result in a fine of up to 200,000 yen and may negatively affect future visa renewals.

2. **Not carrying your Residence Card.** You are legally required to carry your Residence Card at all times. Failure to present it when asked by police or immigration can result in a fine of up to 200,000 yen.

3. **Trying to open a bank account too early.** Walking into a major bank on day one often results in rejection. Use Japan Post Bank or digital alternatives first.

4. **Not getting your address written in Japanese.** Many city hall forms require your address in kanji. Ask your employer, real estate agent, or landlord to write it out for you before you go.

5. **Forgetting to notify your employer of your MyNumber.** Your company needs this promptly for payroll and tax purposes.

6. **Assuming your overseas health insurance is sufficient.** Japan requires enrollment in the Japanese health insurance system. International travel insurance does not satisfy this requirement.

7. **Not keeping your Certificate of Eligibility.** Even after your Residence Card is issued, keep your COE and all immigration documents in a safe place.

8. **Waiting too long for pension enrollment.** Missed months may need to be paid retroactively, and gaps can complicate future claims.

## Key Deadlines

| Action | Deadline | Consequence of Missing |
|--------|----------|----------------------|
| Register address at city hall | Within 14 days of establishing residence | Fine up to 200,000 yen; complications with visa renewal |
| Enroll in health insurance | Immediately after address registration | Retroactive premium charges; uncovered medical expenses |
| Enroll in pension | Immediately after address registration | Back-payment obligations; gaps in record |
| Notify employer of MyNumber | As soon as received | Delays in payroll processing |
| Update Residence Card if address changes | Within 14 days of moving | Fine up to 200,000 yen |
