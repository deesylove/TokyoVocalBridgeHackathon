# Path to Permanent Residency (Eijuken) in Japan

## Overview

Permanent Residency (eijuuken) is the most secure immigration status available in Japan short of citizenship. It removes the restrictions of a specific work visa, eliminates the need for periodic visa renewals, and allows you to work in any field or start your own business. The standard route requires 10 years of continuous residence, but the Highly Skilled Professional points system can shorten this to as little as 1 year. This guide covers all pathways, the application process, and how permanent residency compares to naturalization.

## Who Needs This

- Foreign professionals on work visas who want long-term stability in Japan
- Highly Skilled Professionals who may qualify for an accelerated path (1 or 3 years)
- Spouses of Japanese nationals who may qualify after 3 years of marriage and 1 year of residence
- Long-term residents considering their options between permanent residency and naturalization
- Anyone whose visa will expire and who wants to avoid repeated renewal cycles

## Step-by-Step Process

### Understanding the Eligibility Requirements

#### General Route (10 Years of Residence)

To qualify under the standard pathway, you must meet ALL of the following:

1. **Continuous residence of 10 or more years in Japan.** Within those 10 years, you must have held a work visa or residence visa (not student or trainee) for at least 5 years.
2. **Good conduct.** No criminal record, no significant traffic violations, compliance with tax and social insurance obligations.
3. **Sufficient financial resources.** You must be able to support yourself independently. Immigration generally looks for an annual income of at least 3 million yen, though higher is better, especially if supporting dependents.
4. **Compliance with tax obligations.** You must be current on all tax payments: income tax (shotokuzei), resident tax (juuminzei), and any other applicable taxes. No outstanding amounts.
5. **Compliance with social insurance obligations.** You must be enrolled in and current on health insurance (kenko hoken) and pension (nenkin) payments. This is strictly enforced. Even a single late payment in recent years can cause a denial.
6. **Longest period of stay for your visa category.** You should currently hold the maximum period of stay available for your visa type (typically 5 years for most work visas). If you only have a 1-year or 3-year visa, your application is likely to be denied.
7. **No negative impact on Japan's interests.** A general catch-all that you are not a security concern.

#### Highly Skilled Professional Route (1 or 3 Years)

The **Highly Skilled Foreign Professional (koudou senmonshoku)** points-based system offers a dramatically shortened path:

**Points calculation:** You earn points based on:
- **Academic background:** PhD (30 points), Master's (20 points), Bachelor's (10 points). Degrees from designated top universities earn bonus points.
- **Work experience:** Points increase with years of experience (e.g., 10+ years = 25 points for specialized/technical fields).
- **Annual salary:** Higher salary = more points. For example, earning 10 million yen+ can yield 40 points. The salary thresholds vary by age.
- **Age:** Younger applicants earn more points (under 30 = 15 points, 30-34 = 10 points, 35-39 = 5 points).
- **Bonus points:** Japanese language proficiency (JLPT N1 = 15 points, N2 = 10 points), graduation from a Japanese university, research achievements, position in a company designated by the government, and other factors.

**Point thresholds:**
- **70 points or above:** Eligible for PR after **3 years** of continuous residence in Japan.
- **80 points or above:** Eligible for PR after **1 year** of continuous residence in Japan.

**Important:** You must demonstrate that you had the required points both at the time you started residing in Japan (or when you were granted Highly Skilled Professional status) AND at the time of the PR application. Points are calculated retroactively.

**You do NOT need to formally hold "Highly Skilled Professional" visa status.** Even if you are on a regular Engineer/Specialist in Humanities visa, you can use the points system to demonstrate eligibility as long as you can prove you would have qualified for the Highly Skilled Professional visa.

#### Spouse of Japanese National Route (Shortened)

- **Marriage duration:** 3 or more years of marriage to a Japanese national.
- **Residence:** 1 or more years of continuous residence in Japan.
- Other general requirements (good conduct, financial stability, tax and pension compliance) still apply.

#### Spouse of Permanent Resident Route

- **Marriage duration:** 3 or more years of marriage to a permanent resident.
- **Residence:** 1 or more years of continuous residence in Japan.

#### Long-Term Resident Route

- After 5 years of continuous residence under Long-Term Resident status, you may apply.

### Gathering Required Documents

The document list is extensive. Begin gathering these at least 2-3 months before applying.

**For all applicants:**
1. **Application for Permission for Permanent Residence (eijuu kyoka shinseisho)** -- available at immigration or downloadable from the Immigration Services Agency website.
2. **Passport** -- current and valid.
3. **Residence Card** -- current.
4. **Photo** -- 4cm x 3cm, taken within the last 3 months.
5. **Reason statement (riyuusho)** -- a written explanation of why you are applying for permanent residency. This should be in Japanese (or with a Japanese translation). Explain your ties to Japan, career stability, contributions, and future plans.
6. **Certificate of employment (zaishoku shoumeisho)** -- from your current employer.
7. **Tax certificates:**
   - Income and tax payment certificate (kazei shoumeisho and nouzei shoumeisho) from your municipality for the last 5 years (or 3 years / 1 year for Highly Skilled).
   - Withholding tax slips (gensen choushu hyou) for the last 5 years.
8. **Pension records:**
   - "Nenkin teiki bin" (pension record) or a printout from "Nenkin Net" showing your complete payment history.
   - For National Pension: payment records showing no gaps or late payments for at least the last 2 years. Ideally no late payments at all.
   - For Employee Pension: certificate from your employer or pension office confirming enrollment.
9. **Health insurance records:**
   - For National Health Insurance: payment records showing no gaps for at least the last 2 years.
   - For employer-based insurance: certificate of enrollment from your health insurance society.
10. **Juminhyo (resident record)** -- from your city/ward office, showing household composition.
11. **Letter of guarantee (mimoto hoshousho)** -- from a guarantor who is a Japanese national or permanent resident. The guarantor provides their name, address, occupation, income, and relationship to you, along with their tax and income certificates.

**Additional for Highly Skilled Professional pathway:**
12. **Points calculation sheet** -- the official form showing how you reach 70 or 80 points.
13. **Supporting documents for points claimed:** diplomas, JLPT certificates, employment contracts showing salary, patent certificates, etc.
14. **Evidence that you maintained the required points** throughout the qualifying period (1 or 3 years).

**Additional for spouse of Japanese national:**
15. **Marriage certificate** -- from your country of origin and/or Japan's koseki tohon.
16. **Spouse's koseki tohon (family register)** showing the marriage.

### Submitting the Application

**Where to apply:** Your regional Immigration Services Bureau (nyuukoku kanri kyoku). Not a branch office -- the main regional bureau for your area.

**Process:**
1. Take a number at the permanent residence application counter.
2. Submit all documents. The officer will review them on the spot for completeness.
3. If anything is missing, you will be told what to supplement. It is better to bring everything at once.
4. You will receive a receipt postcard. Keep it safe.
5. **There is no application fee** at the time of submission.
6. When the result is ready, you will receive a notification postcard. Return to immigration with your passport, Residence Card, and the postcard.
7. If approved, pay the **fee of 8,000 yen** (revenue stamp) and receive your new Residence Card showing "Permanent Resident" status.

### Processing Time

- **Standard processing time:** 4 months to over 12 months. The official guideline says 4 months, but in practice, 6-12 months is common.
- **Complex cases:** Can take longer if immigration requests additional documents or clarification.
- **You can check status:** Call the immigration office with your application receipt number.
- **During processing:** Your current visa remains valid. If your visa will expire during processing, you must separately apply for a visa extension. The pending PR application does not automatically extend your current visa.

### If Your Application Is Denied

- You will receive a notification of denial.
- Common reasons: insufficient income, gaps in tax or pension payments, not meeting the residence duration, insufficient points (for Highly Skilled pathway).
- You can reapply immediately. There is no mandatory waiting period. However, it is better to address the reason for denial before reapplying.
- Consider consulting an immigration lawyer (gyousei shoshi) who specializes in immigration cases.

## Advantages of Permanent Residency Over a Work Visa

| Feature | Work Visa | Permanent Residency |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Work restrictions | Limited to visa category scope | No restrictions; any job, any field |
| Visa renewal | Every 1, 3, or 5 years | No renewal needed (Residence Card renewed every 7 years, but PR status itself is indefinite) |
| Business ownership | Requires separate Business Manager visa | Can freely start and run businesses |
| Job loss | Must find new qualifying job or change status | No impact on immigration status |
| Loan and mortgage access | Limited; many banks require PR | Full access to home loans at standard rates |
| Spouse and dependents | Dependents limited to your visa period | Spouse can apply for PR via simplified route |
| Credit card approval | More difficult | Significantly easier |

**Important note on PR and absence from Japan:** PR can be revoked if you leave Japan for more than 1 year without a re-entry permit, or more than 2 years even with a re-entry permit. If you plan extended stays abroad, apply for a re-entry permit (sainyuukoku kyoka) at immigration before departure. The standard re-entry permit is valid for up to 5 years.

## Naturalization as an Alternative

**Naturalization (kika)** means becoming a Japanese citizen. This is a separate process from permanent residency and is handled by the Ministry of Justice (houmusho), not immigration.

**Key differences from PR:**

| Feature | Permanent Residency | Naturalization |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Nationality | Keep your original nationality | Become Japanese; must renounce other nationalities (Japan generally does not allow dual citizenship for adults) |
| Voting rights | None | Full voting and political rights |
| Deportation risk | Can be deported for serious crimes | Cannot be deported |
| Residence requirement | 10 years (or 1-3 via HSP) | 5 years of continuous residence |
| Passport | Keep foreign passport | Japanese passport |
| Application office | Immigration Services Bureau | Legal Affairs Bureau (houmukyoku) |
| Processing time | 4-12 months | 8-18 months |

**Naturalization requirements:**
1. 5 or more years of continuous domicile in Japan.
2. At least 20 years old and of full legal capacity.
3. Good conduct (no criminal record).
4. Ability to support yourself financially (or be supported by spouse/family).
5. Willingness to renounce current nationality.
6. No history of plotting against the Japanese government.
7. Basic Japanese language ability (roughly elementary school level reading and writing).

**When to consider naturalization over PR:**
- You want to vote in Japanese elections.
- You want a Japanese passport (one of the most powerful passports for visa-free travel).
- You want to eliminate any risk of deportation.
- You do not need to maintain your original nationality.

**When to choose PR over naturalization:**
- You want to keep your original nationality and passport.
- You do not want to go through the more intensive naturalization process.
- PR is sufficient for your daily life and career needs.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Late pension payments.** Even one or two late payments in the last 2 years can sink a PR application. Set up automatic payments (jidou furikae) immediately. Immigration checks exact payment dates against due dates.

2. **Not having 5 years of tax and pension records ready.** Start collecting these documents early. Municipal offices may take time to issue multi-year certificates.

3. **Underestimating the income requirement.** While there is no officially published minimum, applications from individuals earning under 3 million yen annually are frequently denied. If you support dependents, aim higher.

4. **Leaving Japan for extended periods.** Long absences (even for business) can break "continuous residence." Generally, a single absence of more than 3 months or cumulative absences exceeding 100 days per year may raise concerns. Each case is evaluated individually.

5. **Not having the maximum visa duration.** If you currently hold a 1-year visa, immigration may view this as a sign that your status is not yet stable enough for PR. Try to obtain a 3-year or 5-year visa before applying.

6. **Weak reason statement.** The riyuusho is your chance to make your case. A vague or perfunctory statement misses the opportunity to demonstrate your commitment to Japan. Mention your career, community involvement, language study, property ownership, family ties, and long-term plans.

7. **Assuming the Highly Skilled points are straightforward.** You must provide evidence for every single point you claim. A diploma for your degree, a certificate for your JLPT score, employment contracts showing your salary history -- all in originals or certified copies with Japanese translations.

8. **Not applying for a visa extension while PR is pending.** If your current visa expires during the PR processing period, you must separately extend it. A pending PR application does not keep your current visa alive.

9. **Choosing a guarantor who cannot demonstrate stable income.** Your guarantor should have a solid income and clean tax record. A guarantor with low income or tax problems weakens your application.

10. **Forgetting that PR requires Residence Card renewal.** PR itself does not expire, but your Residence Card must be renewed every 7 years. Failure to renew the card can lead to complications and fines.

## Key Deadlines

| Action | Deadline | Consequence of Missing |
|--------|----------|----------------------|
| Meet continuous residence requirement | 10 years (general), 3 years (70+ HSP points), 1 year (80+ HSP points) | Cannot apply; must wait |
| Apply for visa extension if current visa expires during PR processing | Before current visa expiration | Overstay status; PR application likely denied |
| Renew Residence Card after receiving PR | Every 7 years | Fine; complications with re-entry |
| Maintain re-entry permit for absences over 1 year | Apply before departure | PR may be revoked |
| Pension and tax payments | Monthly/annually as scheduled | Late payments can cause denial; must be current for at least 2 years |
